The Pursuit of Average

India has the engineers. China has the software. The gap, the reasons, and the early signs it is starting to close.

Author

gp

Published

June 24, 2026

In one breath

India is called a software nation on the strength of some 17 million developers, yet it builds applications on foundations — databases, message queues, kernels — it does not own. China built that foundational layer and India did not, and the reason is not talent but structure: the three mechanisms that produce foundational software barely operate here. The encouraging part is that a transition has begun — scaled winners like Zerodha, PhonePe, and Zoho are descending into the foundations — but mostly from the already-scaled, and the missing half is the garage aimed at a big problem before scale forces it.

Facts and figures here are cited; interpretations are the author’s and are meant to provoke. Contribution data is partial — counts are proxies, not censuses. Errors of fact should be corrected; errors of opinion are on purpose.

The Claim, and the Word It Hides Behind

India is a software nation. This is repeated in boardrooms and budget speeches with the calm of a fact that has never been asked to prove itself, and by one measure it survives the asking: as of late 2025, roughly 17 million Indian developers on GitHub, the second-largest population on the platform, growing fastest, due to overtake the United States by 2028.1 Seventeen million is real. It is also the wrong number to be proud of.

"Software nation" smuggles in a word it never defines. There is application software — the app, the website, the dashboard that emails you when a server sulks — and there is foundational software: the database the app writes to, the operating system it runs on, the message queue it leans on, the cryptography between it and the morning headlines. The application is what the user sees. The foundation is what the application stands on, generally without thinking about it, the way one does not think about the floor until it is gone.

India builds applications. With a small and instructive set of exceptions, it does not build foundations. China does. This is the actual situation under the comfortable cliché that China makes hardware and India makes software — a sentence half right in the way that is more dangerous than being wrong outright.

The argument, as a pyramid

This essay is built as an argument pyramid: a single claim at the top, resting on layers of narrower claims, each resting on evidence, several levels deep. Only the top two levels are drawn here; the rest is the prose that follows.

India is celebrated for software, yet builds on foundations it does not own. The gap with China is structural, not a matter of talent — and it is closable.

The claim is miscategorised: application, not foundation.

China’s stack is categorically deeper, adopted on merit.

Foundations are born of three mechanisms India barely runs.

A transition is underway, but the garage must aim at big problems.

The standard answer to "why doesn't India build foundations" reaches for talent. The standard answer is wrong. The real answer is structural, and a structure can be changed.

Two Phenotypes in One Browser

Foundational software is born two ways, and both are running in a program you probably have open. Visit chrome://version or edge://version and consider what is underneath.

LevelDB sits inside the browser's IndexedDB and several internal databases. It was written in 2011 by Jeff Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat — two of the most decorated engineers alive — at Google, to solve a problem Google had.2 It is excellent, and it is institutional software: abundant resources, an explicit mandate, two people whose careers were in no danger. The open-source release came afterwards, the way one decides what to do with leftovers.

SQLite sits elsewhere in the same process — the cookie store, the history, the preferences. It was begun in 2000 by D. Richard Hipp, alone, under a United States Navy contract to track equipment aboard a guided-missile destroyer.3 When the contract ended he kept going, and placed the result in the public domain — no licence, no foundation, no company — whereupon it became the most widely deployed database engine in history: every phone, every browser, Airbus avionics, every copy of WhatsApp on Earth.4 One person, because the problem was interesting and the existing tools were wrong.

Where most projects keep a licence, SQLite keeps a blessing, visible in its own source:5

May you do good and not evil. May you find forgiveness for yourself and forgive others. May you share freely, never taking more than you give.

Two pieces of foundational software, in one binary, neither having displaced the other because they were never competing. One is what a powerful institution produces on purpose. The other is what a single person produces because they cannot help it. A country that builds neither kind is missing two distinct mechanisms — and the rest of this essay is really one question: which of them does India run, and which does it quietly suppress?

A named absence: the gateway and identity layer

It is worth dwelling on one layer, because the absence there is total and the market is crowded. The API gateway — the traffic cop that sits in front of every modern microservice — has a deep field of open-source and commercial options: Kong from the United States, Apigee now inside Google, APISIX from China, Tyk from the United Kingdom, MuleSoft inside Salesforce, Gravitee from France. Identity and access management is similar: Keycloak from Red Hat, Ory's Kratos and Hydra from Germany, Auth0 now inside Okta. Across this entire layer — the gates and the locks of the internet — India has built nothing that the world runs. The one Indian name in the vicinity, Postman, sits a storey above, at tooling, not at the gateway itself, and we will come to why that distinction matters.

How Foundational Software Is Actually Born

If China has it and India does not, the useful question is mechanical: by what process does foundational software come to exist? The record is consistent and points to three mechanisms.

The iconoclast decides the existing tool is wrong at a level no configuration can fix, and rebuilds it. SQLite is the archetype; Gitea, begun by one developer in 2014, is the same impulse with Chinese characteristics; Redpanda, which we will meet again, is the same bet against Kafka.

The domain expert who learns to code is the most underrated of the three, and has quietly produced much of the scientific stack.

A psychiatrist writes the world’s neuroimaging standard

Karl Friston is, by training, a psychiatrist. In the early 1990s, doing brain imaging at the Wellcome Trust, he needed statistical analyses of PET and MRI scans that no software could perform — so he wrote them. SPM became, and remains, the dominant package in human neuroimaging; a vast share of every brain-imaging paper of the last thirty years rests on software written by a doctor who declined to wait for a tool.[fn:spm]

He is not a fluke. R came from two statisticians wanting a better way to teach; QGIS from a geographer who wanted a GIS that ran on Linux; OpenFOAM from mechanical engineers; BLAS, under nearly all numerical computing, from numerical analysts doing physics.11 In every case the generality was an accident of depth. Nobody set out to build foundational infrastructure; they had a problem too specific for the available tools, and built it right.

The third mechanism is the cross-disciplinary room — the institute where a problem and a codebase are shared across fields. CERN's byproducts include the World Wide Web.12 And the cleanest modern example of this mechanism is not in India, which is the uncomfortable part.

The lone star — and it is not India

The University of Moratuwa, in Sri Lanka, runs a computer-science research group whose work became foundational software the world depends on. Sanjiva Weerawarana — Purdue PhD, eight years at IBM Research, a visiting professor at Moratuwa who chose to stay in Colombo rather than relocate to San Jose — co-founded WSO2 in 2005.[fn:wso2] Out of the Moratuwa research orbit, led by Srinath Perera, came Siddhi: a stream-processing and complex-event-processing engine that became the runtime inside WSO2’s products and is deployed for real-time work at the scale of Uber’s fraud detection — a use confirmed, with some delight, in Weerawarana’s own account of moonlighting as an Uber driver.[fn:siddhi] WSO2 also ships an API manager, an identity server, and Ballerina, a programming language purpose-built for integration.

Sri Lanka has 22 million people and no software-nation mythology whatsoever. One university research group there produced a globally-deployed streaming engine. The IITs produce more computer-science graduates in a year than Moratuwa has in its history; Moratuwa has the engine. The mechanism is not population. It is the room where a problem and a codebase are shared, and a professor who stayed.

There is a fourth thing, not a mechanism but a norm: how the work is shared. Foundational software accretes a community when its makers publish the reasoning openly, before it is finished.

What sharing the work looks like

Anthropic’s interpretability group publishes its research — including dead ends and half-formed results — openly on the Transformer Circuits thread, in the open, as it goes.[fn:tc] This is not foundational software and it is not Indian; it is the norm the rest of this essay keeps pointing at: the habit of showing the work, which converts a private insight into a thing others can build on. It is exactly the connective tissue the lone Indian builder, later in this story, does not have.

China runs all three mechanisms and the norm. Sri Lanka, on the evidence of one professor, runs the third. The question for India is how many of them its system permits.

What Has, and Has Not, Happened Here

India's real contribution is genuine and lives at the protocol layer. The India Stack — Aadhaar, UPI, ONDC — is a set of open specifications on which private players compete; UPI clears over 14 billion transactions a month and has been copied from Singapore to Brazil.13 But it must be described precisely, because imprecision is where self-congratulation lives: UPI is a specification, not deployable code, and PhonePe, Google Pay, and Paytm built proprietary implementations on top of it. The cleanest exception is MOSIP, the open-source identity platform from IIIT-Bangalore, now running in fourteen countries — India's clearest single export of foundational open-source software, and tellingly never Apache-incubated.14

The most important recent development is also the most inconvenient for the thesis, and honesty requires stating it at full strength.

Sarvam — the strongest recent release, and why it is still not a foundation

In February 2026, Sarvam AI — founded by two AI4Bharat alumni from IIT Madras — released Sarvam-30B and a 105-billion-parameter model, trained from scratch on Indian-language data and open-sourced under Apache-2.0 on Hugging Face.[fn:sarvam] It is the closest India has come to the Chinese pattern: a real constraint, a build from first principles, an open release under the global-adoption licence.

And yet a trained model is not foundational software in the sense this essay uses everywhere else. A database, a queue, a kernel are infrastructure that other software runs on; a model is an artefact produced by running that infrastructure — and Sarvam’s trains and serves, with some irony, on American and Chinese foundations. It is an open model, not an open foundation. Krutrim, the other flagship, is weaker still: models shipped, then senior leaders and clarity shed in roughly equal measure.[fn:krutrim] The model layer is a genuine stirring. It is not, yet, the floor.

The iconoclast does fire here — rarely, unsupported, which is what makes it clarifying. It appears as foundational software built for love, from a real need.

Listmonk, and a language in code

Kailash Nadh, who works at the brokerage Zerodha, needed a high-performance self-hosted mailing-list manager, found none adequate, and built one — Listmonk, MIT-licensed, now used worldwide.[fn:listmonk] Santhosh Thottingal has spent years on the foundational layer of a whole language: Malayalam fonts, a morphological analyser, a hyphenation dictionary — the sort of thing nobody notices until it is missing and text stops rendering.[fn:thottingal] He is a product of the ecosystem that appeared when Kerala, constrained by budget rather than ideology, put open-source software in its schools in the early 2000s. Both are a labour of love born from a need and an interest.

There is also a genuine Indian success in this neighbourhood, and it is worth being exact about where it sits.

Postman — a real win, one storey above the foundation

Postman began in Bangalore in 2014 — Abhinav Asthana, Ankit Sobti, and Abhijit Kane, starting from a humble Chrome extension — and became the global standard for API testing and collaboration, used by tens of millions of developers and most of the Fortune 500.[fn:postman] This is a real and large success. It is also, precisely, a developer tool, not an API gateway or a piece of infrastructure: India’s most globally-adopted API product sits one storey above the gateway, and the gateway itself is Chinese (APISIX) or American (Kong, Apigee). This is not a criticism of Postman. It is an accurate map of where Indian builders have succeeded — the application and tooling layer — and where they have not.

And then there is the mechanism that, in India, mostly does not fire at all.

A personal note

Someone I know is building a messaging system from the ground up — not a wrapper around Kafka, a redesign that starts from different premises about ordering and about what “delivery” means when a consumer can be arbitrarily slow. The argument is coherent, the code exists, the benchmarks are being written. What does not exist around it is everything else: runway, an early-adopter community, a blog that makes the problem legible, a conference slot. Redpanda made the same bet — that Kafka is wrong at the architecture level — and is now in production at thousands of companies.[fn:redpanda] The difference is not the insight. It is access to the connective tissue that turns a correct idea into a project with a community.

The other two mechanisms barely fire. There is no Indian SPM, no Indian R, no Indian CERN. The exception that proves the rule is AI4Bharat at IIT Madras — the one genuinely cross-disciplinary effort, whose alumni founded Sarvam. The single working instance of mechanism three produced the single counterexample. The sample size is one, and it behaves exactly as the theory predicts.

Why — The Pursuit of Average

So why, with the second-largest developer population on Earth, does India build at the top of the stack and almost never at the bottom? The seventeen million is the clue, not the rebuttal: headcount and foundational output are different axes, and the whole confusion is the act of treating one as a proxy for the other.

India sits far right on people and near the floor on foundations; Germany and the UK, with a fraction of the developers, sit well above it. The talent is present and something else is absent. Four structural reasons account for the something.

There is no forcing function. China built OceanBase because Singles' Day broke Oracle and money could not un-break it.15 India's industry was built as a services-export business writing code for Western clients on Western infrastructure, very well and very profitably; there was no domestic scale problem that broke a purchased database, and data-localisation rules are satisfied by running in AWS Mumbai. The pressure that manufactures foundational software was never applied.

There is the jugaad trap. Indian engineering venerates the frugal workaround assembled from existing parts,16 which is admirable at the product layer and the exact opposite of the foundational impulse: jugaad celebrates the existing foundation and builds cleverly atop it, where SQLite and OceanBase exist because someone judged the foundation wrong and rebuilt it. A culture that rewards the workaround over the rebuild produces applications. It is doing precisely what it is optimised to do.

There is the silo, and the price of failure. The domain-expert mechanism needs a doctor or a geographer to be able to learn to code — and the system to tolerate the time. Indian professional education is unusually siloed, and the failure cost is asymmetric: elsewhere a researcher who spends two years on a tool that fails loses two years; here they can lose their standing, funding, and career. The mechanism that produced SPM requires permission to fail at the primary task, and the system declines to grant it.

And there is the feedback loop that never closed — the most instructive of the four, because it shows the missing ingredient is not what people assume.

BARC did the documentation right, and it still wasn’t enough

The usual story is that India’s state computing failed for want of documentation. It is more uncomfortable than that. India’s Department of Atomic Energy, through BARC, built more than twenty ANUPAM supercomputers from 1991 onward and documented them properly — in peer-reviewed papers, in Springer volumes, in the scientific record.[fn:barc] C-DAC’s PARAM, by contrast, lives mostly in press releases. Yet neither produced reusable open software, and BARC’s good documentation did not save it, because ANUPAM was built for internal reactor physics — there was no external user to pull the work outward, fork it, demand it improve. The missing ingredient was never the blog. It was the downstream user.

The same lesson, generalised, separates India's state programs from the comparators that worked.

C-DAC also lost on procurement, where specifications were quietly written to match the incumbent foreign vendor, and on retention, where the services boom tripled salaries and the capability walked out, leaving process behind. OceanBase improved because Alipay's load broke it in public; ANUPAM and PARAM faced no such pressure and calcified.

And when the talent did build foundational software, it often did so elsewhere, for someone else.

Apigee — the export, named

The API-management company that became Apigee began as Sonoa Systems in 2004 in Santa Clara, founded by Raj Singh — an engineer of Indian origin, from Meerut — and Ravi Chandra; Chet Kapoor joined as CEO in 2006 and later took it public. Google bought it in 2016 for about 625 million dollars.[fn:apigee] An engineer with an Indian name built foundational API infrastructure that twenty of the Fortune 100 came to depend on. He built it in San Jose, on American capital, for an American acquirer. The talent was not absent. The system that would have kept it in India was. This is the most concrete instance of the talent being exported at a discount.

The composite is fairly called a pursuit of the average — not an insult to individuals, many of them world-class, but a description of what the system selects for. The safe path is the services job, the application on someone else's cloud, the workaround admired in the standup. The foundational build — slow, uncertain, demanding both a tolerance for failure and a serene indifference to the premium career — is exactly the profile the incentive structure filters out before the interview.

The Transition Is Real — and Going the Wrong Way

Here the story turns, because something genuinely new is happening. The Indian companies that survived and scaled are now descending into the foundational layer — building the floor because the borrowed one finally stopped fitting. And one company went there on principle, before it had to.

Zerodha — the model already working

A thirty-person tech team runs roughly a fifth of India’s stock trades, about fourteen million trades on a record day, at sub-forty-millisecond mean latency, on self-hosted open source with no vendor lock-in.[fn:zerodha-s] They open-source what they build and run it themselves — DungBeetle, a distributed SQL job server; kaf-relay, for Kafka replication; Listmonk — and they co-founded FOSS United and put a million dollars a year into a FOSS fund.[fn:zerodha-o] Their own blog states the disposition plainly: “Nobody has any background in finance… everyone has been a hacker or a hobbyist straight out of college. What colleges, neither do we ask, nor do we keep a track of.”[fn:zerodha-blog] When a four-decade, single-handed Kannada dictionary surfaced, it was discovered and open-sourced not by a university but by this brokerage. It is the iconoclast and the domain expert and the connective tissue, in one company.

Drove — built, opened, and waiting for an audience

PhonePe — a payments company — built its own container orchestrator, Drove, as a declared successor to Apache Mesos/Marathon, and a bare-metal provisioner, Nimbus, on custom iPXE.[fn:drove] Drove is genuinely open-sourced, documented, and was presented for technical review at Rootconf in early 2025. It is real, it runs PhonePe’s production infrastructure — and it is almost entirely un-adopted outside PhonePe. There is no chorus of outside companies, no “we switched from Kubernetes to Drove” write-ups. This is the new failure mode: not built-but-not-opened, but built, opened, and left at the edge of adoption for want of the unglamorous evangelism — the benchmarks, the outsider documentation, the marketing — that turns a published repository into a standard.

Frappe — the unglamorous birth

India’s one genuine application-framework export began without ceremony. Frappe’s ERPNext was licensed under the GPL and sat on Google Code from 2009; its founder rewrote it, by his own account, “a couple of times,” and in the early days it had “no clear deployment instructions, very sparse documentation and no community forum.”[fn:frappe] From that unpromising start it grew a forum now past twenty-two thousand members. Foundational-ish software, born ugly, kept alive by persistence.

Zoho — the descent made on principle

Zoho is the outlier that matters most. Founded in 1996 as AdventNet by Sridhar Vembu — IIT Madras, a Princeton PhD, ex-Qualcomm, who returned not to Bangalore but to the town of Tenkasi in rural Tamil Nadu — it bootstrapped to over a hundred million users and well past a billion dollars in revenue without ever taking venture capital, and is still private.[fn:zoho-sv] Its first product, in the 1990s, was WebNMS, a network-management framework sold to telcos worldwide — infrastructure, not an application, from the very start.[fn:webnms] It built its own scripting language, Deluge, to run Zoho Creator and Flow; an owned language is an owned layer. It shipped Ulaa, a Chromium-based browser whose DNS routes through India’s national root servers.[fn:ulaa] And, most strikingly, Zoho does not rent its foundation: its products run on hardware Zoho owns and software frameworks Zoho wrote, atop Linux and Postgres, in its own data centres — not on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud — and in 2026 it unveiled Nathu La, a server motherboard and chassis it designed in-house, for sovereignty and to cut inference cost.[fn:zoho-server]

Every other company in this story descended into the foundations because scale forced it. Zoho descended because Vembu decided it was the right thing to do — before the scale arrived, before the database broke, before anyone called it necessary. Zoho is not open source, and the thesis holds regardless: the issue is foundational work, not licensing. Zoho is the proof that the decision to own the floor can be made on principle, years before economics demands it.

This descent is real, recent, and the right direction. But for the most part it is a privilege of the already-scaled: it flows downward, only after winning at scale, and each winner builds only the few pieces its own problem demands. That is a structural ceiling. Few companies reach that scale, so the country gets few foundational projects, each narrow.

The encouraging exception to the ceiling is FOSS United — co-founded by Zerodha — which is the closest thing India has to the connective tissue this essay keeps naming as missing: a network, conferences, a fund, the beginnings of a place where a builder in one town can discover the builder in another who solved the same problem last year. It is the right bridge, half-built.

What India still does not have is the other half: the garage aimed at a genuinely big problem before scale forces it. The lone builder normally has a small problem an existing library handles, so jugaad is rational and they stay at the top of the stack. The SQLite phenotype — the small builder going deep before anyone has said it is worth money — is precisely the thing the descent of the scaled winners cannot supply, and it is the thing the country most lacks.

A Way Out That Does Not Run Through the IITs

The reflexive prescription points at the IITs. It is almost certainly wrong: their graduates have the best options in the country, and the student who builds a distributed database is declining a comfortable offer to do something hard with an uncertain payoff. The elite institution has the talent and exactly the wrong incentives.

A better target is the engineer at a B-tier polytechnic in a tier-two city — strong fundamentals, fewer escape routes, and proximity to a real problem no existing software solves. Picture a garment cluster like Tiruppur, or a mining-transport hub. A local transporter — a dozen trucks, family-run — has a routing problem whose addresses mix Tamil script, transliteration, and informal landmarks that Google Maps treats with contempt. No enterprise software addresses it at a price the transporter can pay, because the market is too small to interest anyone in California. The nearby polytechnic has students who need projects. Every part of the forcing function is already in the room: a real problem, no budget, local capability, and an economics that makes proprietary software impossible — so the output is open by default, not from virtue but because there is nobody to sell it to.

Kerala already ran this experiment by accident: a budget-driven decision to put open-source software in schools produced, two decades on, a generation of foundational contributors. No grand vision was required, and none was present.

And one company has been running the talent half of this experiment on purpose for two decades. Zoho hires without degrees, from tier-two and tier-three towns, and trains its people internally through Zoho Schools; its headquarters is a village, and the village is treated as the asset rather than the handicap. The experiment this essay asks someone to run — strong local talent, a real problem, freedom from the premium- career pull — is the experiment Zoho has been quietly running since the mid-2000s, and it is no coincidence that the same company owns its stack from the server motherboard to the browser.

Alongside the organic, a directed programme on the DARPA and 863 pattern — not research grants, which buy papers, but engineering contracts, which buy software, with named deliverables and dates. Ten named problems: a distributed database tuned for UPI-scale transaction graphs, a production-grade Indian-language morphological analyser, an open pipeline turning ISRO imagery into per-field guidance for a farmer, a genomics toolkit calibrated for Indian population structure rather than borrowed European references.17 Run them at regional institutions, because the constraint is the asset.

And the two bridges, stated plainly, because they are the whole fix: a forcing function for the garage, so the small builder meets a big problem before scale would otherwise force it; and a marketing-and-adoption layer for the descenders, so that a Drove becomes a shared foundation rather than a private one. The pressures that might supply the first are already assembling on their own — the data-localisation provisions of the DPDP Act, the RBI's circulars on cloud concentration, the public compute of the IndiaAI Mission that produced Sarvam. Whether anyone names them and points them at the infrastructure layers is a choice, and it has not yet been made.

Early Signs, and an Honest Close

India writes a staggering quantity of software and is not, with a thin and widening set of exceptions, a foundational software nation. China is. The gap appears the moment one stops counting developers and starts counting databases and message queues, and it is not a gap of talent — the engineers who built much of the world's foundational infrastructure trained here and built it for American companies, on American payrolls. Apigee is the named, specific exemplar: the talent was exported at a discount.

But the close is not despair, because the early signs are real. The scaled winners are descending into the foundations — Zerodha, PhonePe, Frappe — and Zoho went there on principle before scale forced it. Zerodha is building the connective tissue the country lacked. Sarvam has done something genuinely open, even if it is a model and not yet a floor. The descent has begun. What has not begun is the ascent — the garage going deep before scale forces it — and that is the half that produces the SQLites, because the SQLite phenotype is by definition the small builder attacking a deep problem before anyone has told them it is worth money.

The dedication in SQLite's source is not a policy instrument. Share freely, never taking more than you give is a disposition, and dispositions cannot be issued as an order. But the conditions under which that disposition turns into software can be deliberately built, and India has not built them at scale. That is a choice — and, unlike talent, it is one the country still gets to make, while the window is open and Sarvam is still the exception rather than the proof.

Further Areas of Interest

This essay opens more threads than one piece can pursue. Each of the following is hinted at above and deserves its own treatment.

  • The licence as geopolitics. Apache-2.0 versus MulanPubL-2.0 was treated here as a signal of intent; a fuller study would map how licence choice tracks the ambition to be adopted globally versus the obligation to comply domestically — and what that implies for a Western enterprise's legal review of any China-origin tool.

  • The Apache Foundation as trust machinery. Fourteen Chinese projects routed through the ASF; MOSIP, India's cleanest export, did not. Why incubation at a neutral foundation functions as an internationalisation step, and why Indian projects skip it, is a question this essay only gestures at.

  • The Sri Lanka comparison, properly. WSO2 and Siddhi suggest a 22-million-person country ran the cross-disciplinary mechanism India did not. What did the University of Moratuwa do — institutionally — that Indian engineering colleges have not, and is it transferable?

  • The model layer's trajectory. Sarvam is, for now, an open model rather than an open foundation. Whether the IndiaAI cohort (Sarvam, BharatGen, Soket AI, and others) descends into the infrastructure beneath the models — training stacks, serving engines, inference runtimes — is the single most important thing to watch over the next three years.

  • Zoho as a controlled experiment. A company that owns its stack from an in-house server motherboard to a browser, hires from villages without degrees, and refuses outside capital is a natural experiment in nearly every prescription this essay makes. Its successes and its limits both deserve close, unsentimental study — including where vertical integration becomes a constraint rather than a strength.

  • The marketing-and-adoption gap. Drove is built, opened, and largely un-adopted. The unglamorous discipline that turns a published repository into a standard — benchmarks, outsider documentation, conference advocacy, semantic-versioning discipline — is itself a capability India underinvests in, and it would repay a dedicated study of what successful open-source go-to-market actually requires.

  • The protocol-to-code question. ONDC ships reference implementations; Zoho is in talks with iSpirt to publish an open messaging protocol for Arattai. Whether India's genuine strength at the protocol layer can be deliberately converted into deployable foundational code is perhaps the most actionable open question of all.

  • The returning diaspora. Apigee's foundational work happened in San Jose. With visa friction rising and remote work normalised, some of that talent is returning or building from India. Whether the system can now retain what it once exported is a live, testable proposition.

# The Claim, and the Word It Hides Behind {#claim}

India is a software nation. This is repeated in boardrooms and budget
speeches with the calm of a fact that has never been asked to prove
itself, and by one measure it survives the asking: as of late 2025,
roughly 17 million Indian developers on GitHub, the second-largest
population on the platform, growing fastest, due to overtake the United
States by 2028.[^1] Seventeen million is real. It is also the wrong
number to be proud of.

\"Software nation\" smuggles in a word it never defines. There is
application software --- the app, the website, the dashboard that emails
you when a server sulks --- and there is foundational software: the
database the app writes to, the operating system it runs on, the message
queue it leans on, the cryptography between it and the morning
headlines. The application is what the user sees. The foundation is what
the application stands on, generally without thinking about it, the way
one does not think about the floor until it is gone.

India builds applications. With a small and instructive set of
exceptions, it does not build foundations. China does. This is the
actual situation under the comfortable cliché that China makes hardware
and India makes software --- a sentence half right in the way that is
more dangerous than being wrong outright.

The standard answer to \"why doesn\'t India build foundations\" reaches
for talent. The standard answer is wrong. The real answer is structural,
and a structure can be changed.

# Two Phenotypes in One Browser {#phenotypes}

Foundational software is born two ways, and both are running in a
program you probably have open. Visit `chrome://version` or
`edge://version` and consider what is underneath.

LevelDB sits inside the browser\'s IndexedDB and several internal
databases. It was written in 2011 by Jeff Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat ---
two of the most decorated engineers alive --- at Google, to solve a
problem Google had.[^2] It is excellent, and it is *institutional*
software: abundant resources, an explicit mandate, two people whose
careers were in no danger. The open-source release came afterwards, the
way one decides what to do with leftovers.

SQLite sits elsewhere in the same process --- the cookie store, the
history, the preferences. It was begun in 2000 by D. Richard Hipp,
alone, under a United States Navy contract to track equipment aboard a
guided-missile destroyer.[^3] When the contract ended he kept going, and
placed the result in the public domain --- no licence, no foundation, no
company --- whereupon it became the most widely deployed database engine
in history: every phone, every browser, Airbus avionics, every copy of
WhatsApp on Earth.[^4] One person, because the problem was interesting
and the existing tools were wrong.

Where most projects keep a licence, SQLite keeps a blessing, visible in
its own source:[^5]

> May you do good and not evil. May you find forgiveness for yourself
> and forgive others. May you share freely, never taking more than you
> give.

Two pieces of foundational software, in one binary, neither having
displaced the other because they were never competing. One is what a
powerful institution produces on purpose. The other is what a single
person produces because they cannot help it. A country that builds
neither kind is missing two distinct mechanisms --- and the rest of this
essay is really one question: which of them does India run, and which
does it quietly suppress?

## A named absence: the gateway and identity layer

It is worth dwelling on one layer, because the absence there is total
and the market is crowded. The API gateway --- the traffic cop that sits
in front of every modern microservice --- has a deep field of
open-source and commercial options: Kong from the United States, Apigee
now inside Google, APISIX from China, Tyk from the United Kingdom,
MuleSoft inside Salesforce, Gravitee from France. Identity and access
management is similar: Keycloak from Red Hat, Ory\'s Kratos and Hydra
from Germany, Auth0 now inside Okta. Across this entire layer --- the
gates and the locks of the internet --- India has built nothing that the
world runs. The one Indian name in the vicinity, Postman, sits a storey
above, at tooling, not at the gateway itself, and we will come to why
that distinction matters.

# How Foundational Software Is Actually Born {#mechanisms}

If China has it and India does not, the useful question is mechanical:
by what process does foundational software come to exist? The record is
consistent and points to three mechanisms.

The **iconoclast** decides the existing tool is wrong at a level no
configuration can fix, and rebuilds it. SQLite is the archetype; Gitea,
begun by one developer in 2014, is the same impulse with Chinese
characteristics; Redpanda, which we will meet again, is the same bet
against Kafka.

The **domain expert who learns to code** is the most underrated of the
three, and has quietly produced much of the scientific stack.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">A psychiatrist writes the world's neuroimaging standard</div>
<p>
Karl Friston is, by training, a psychiatrist. In the early 1990s, doing brain
imaging at the Wellcome Trust, he needed statistical analyses of PET and MRI scans
that no software could perform — so he wrote them. SPM became, and remains, the
dominant package in human neuroimaging; a vast share of every brain-imaging paper
of the last thirty years rests on software written by a doctor who declined to
wait for a tool.[fn:spm]
</p>
</div>

He is not a fluke. R came from two statisticians wanting a better way to
teach; QGIS from a geographer who wanted a GIS that ran on Linux;
OpenFOAM from mechanical engineers; BLAS, under nearly all numerical
computing, from numerical analysts doing physics.[^11] In every case the
generality was an accident of depth. Nobody set out to build
foundational infrastructure; they had a problem too specific for the
available tools, and built it right.

  Tool       Domain           Author\'s training
  ---------- ---------------- ----------------------
  SPM        Neuroimaging     Psychiatrist
  R          Statistics       Statisticians
  QGIS       Geospatial       Geographer
  OpenFOAM   Fluid dynamics   Mechanical engineers
  BLAS       Linear algebra   Numerical analysts

The third mechanism is the **cross-disciplinary room** --- the institute
where a problem and a codebase are shared across fields. CERN\'s
byproducts include the World Wide Web.[^12] And the cleanest modern
example of this mechanism is not in India, which is the uncomfortable
part.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">The lone star — and it is not India</div>
<p>
The University of Moratuwa, in Sri Lanka, runs a computer-science research group
whose work became foundational software the world depends on. Sanjiva Weerawarana
— Purdue PhD, eight years at IBM Research, a visiting professor at Moratuwa who
chose to stay in Colombo rather than relocate to San Jose — co-founded WSO2 in
2005.[fn:wso2] Out of the Moratuwa research orbit, led by Srinath Perera, came
Siddhi: a stream-processing and complex-event-processing engine that became the
runtime inside WSO2's products and is deployed for real-time work at the scale of
Uber's fraud detection — a use confirmed, with some delight, in Weerawarana's own
account of moonlighting as an Uber driver.[fn:siddhi] WSO2 also ships an API
manager, an identity server, and Ballerina, a programming language purpose-built
for integration.
</p>
<p>
Sri Lanka has 22 million people and no software-nation mythology whatsoever. One
university research group there produced a globally-deployed streaming engine. The
IITs produce more computer-science graduates in a year than Moratuwa has in its
history; Moratuwa has the engine. The mechanism is not population. It is the room
where a problem and a codebase are shared, and a professor who stayed.
</p>
</div>

There is a fourth thing, not a mechanism but a norm: how the work is
shared. Foundational software accretes a community when its makers
publish the reasoning openly, before it is finished.

<div class="story-box story-box-counter">
<div class="story-label">What sharing the work looks like</div>
<p>
Anthropic's interpretability group publishes its research — including dead ends and
half-formed results — openly on the Transformer Circuits thread, in the open, as it
goes.[fn:tc] This is not foundational software and it is not Indian; it is the
<em>norm</em> the rest of this essay keeps pointing at: the habit of showing the
work, which converts a private insight into a thing others can build on. It is
exactly the connective tissue the lone Indian builder, later in this story, does
not have.
</p>
</div>

China runs all three mechanisms and the norm. Sri Lanka, on the evidence
of one professor, runs the third. The question for India is how many of
them its system permits.

# What Has, and Has Not, Happened Here {#india}

India\'s real contribution is genuine and lives at the protocol layer.
The India Stack --- Aadhaar, UPI, ONDC --- is a set of open
specifications on which private players compete; UPI clears over 14
billion transactions a month and has been copied from Singapore to
Brazil.[^13] But it must be described precisely, because imprecision is
where self-congratulation lives: UPI is a specification, not deployable
code, and PhonePe, Google Pay, and Paytm built proprietary
implementations on top of it. The cleanest exception is MOSIP, the
open-source identity platform from IIIT-Bangalore, now running in
fourteen countries --- India\'s clearest single export of foundational
open-source software, and tellingly never Apache-incubated.[^14]

The most important recent development is also the most inconvenient for
the thesis, and honesty requires stating it at full strength.

<div class="story-box story-box-counter">
<div class="story-label">Sarvam — the strongest recent release, and why it is still not a foundation</div>
<p>
In February 2026, Sarvam AI — founded by two AI4Bharat alumni from IIT Madras —
released Sarvam-30B and a 105-billion-parameter model, trained from scratch on
Indian-language data and open-sourced under Apache-2.0 on Hugging Face.[fn:sarvam]
It is the closest India has come to the Chinese pattern: a real constraint, a build
from first principles, an open release under the global-adoption licence.
</p>
<p>
And yet a trained model is not foundational software in the sense this essay uses
everywhere else. A database, a queue, a kernel are infrastructure that other
software runs <em>on</em>; a model is an artefact <em>produced by</em> running that
infrastructure — and Sarvam's trains and serves, with some irony, on American and
Chinese foundations. It is an open model, not an open foundation. Krutrim, the
other flagship, is weaker still: models shipped, then senior leaders and clarity
shed in roughly equal measure.[fn:krutrim] The model layer is a genuine stirring.
It is not, yet, the floor.
</p>
</div>

The iconoclast does fire here --- rarely, unsupported, which is what
makes it clarifying. It appears as foundational software built for love,
from a real need.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Listmonk, and a language in code</div>
<p>
Kailash Nadh, who works at the brokerage Zerodha, needed a high-performance
self-hosted mailing-list manager, found none adequate, and built one — Listmonk,
MIT-licensed, now used worldwide.[fn:listmonk] Santhosh Thottingal has spent years
on the foundational layer of a whole language: Malayalam fonts, a morphological
analyser, a hyphenation dictionary — the sort of thing nobody notices until it is
missing and text stops rendering.[fn:thottingal] He is a product of the ecosystem
that appeared when Kerala, constrained by budget rather than ideology, put
open-source software in its schools in the early 2000s. Both are a labour of love
born from a need and an interest.
</p>
</div>

There is also a genuine Indian success in this neighbourhood, and it is
worth being exact about where it sits.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Postman — a real win, one storey above the foundation</div>
<p>
Postman began in Bangalore in 2014 — Abhinav Asthana, Ankit Sobti, and Abhijit Kane,
starting from a humble Chrome extension — and became the global standard for API
testing and collaboration, used by tens of millions of developers and most of the
Fortune 500.[fn:postman] This is a real and large success. It is also, precisely, a
developer tool, not an API gateway or a piece of infrastructure: India's most
globally-adopted API product sits one storey above the gateway, and the gateway
itself is Chinese (APISIX) or American (Kong, Apigee). This is not a criticism of
Postman. It is an accurate map of where Indian builders have succeeded — the
application and tooling layer — and where they have not.
</p>
</div>

And then there is the mechanism that, in India, mostly does not fire at
all.

<div class="anecdote-box">
<div class="anecdote-label">A personal note</div>
<p>
Someone I know is building a messaging system from the ground up — not a wrapper
around Kafka, a redesign that starts from different premises about ordering and
about what "delivery" means when a consumer can be arbitrarily slow. The argument is
coherent, the code exists, the benchmarks are being written. What does not exist
around it is everything else: runway, an early-adopter community, a blog that makes
the problem legible, a conference slot. Redpanda made the same bet — that Kafka is
wrong at the architecture level — and is now in production at thousands of
companies.[fn:redpanda] The difference is not the insight. It is access to the
connective tissue that turns a correct idea into a project with a community.
</p>
</div>

The other two mechanisms barely fire. There is no Indian SPM, no Indian
R, no Indian CERN. The exception that proves the rule is AI4Bharat at
IIT Madras --- the one genuinely cross-disciplinary effort, whose alumni
founded Sarvam. The single working instance of mechanism three produced
the single counterexample. The sample size is one, and it behaves
exactly as the theory predicts.

# Why --- The Pursuit of Average {#why}

So why, with the second-largest developer population on Earth, does
India build at the top of the stack and almost never at the bottom? The
seventeen million is the clue, not the rebuttal: headcount and
foundational output are different axes, and the whole confusion is the
act of treating one as a proxy for the other.

India sits far right on people and near the floor on foundations;
Germany and the UK, with a fraction of the developers, sit well above
it. The talent is present and something else is absent. Four structural
reasons account for the something.

There is **no forcing function**. China built OceanBase because
Singles\' Day broke Oracle and money could not un-break it.[^15]
India\'s industry was built as a services-export business writing code
for Western clients on Western infrastructure, very well and very
profitably; there was no domestic scale problem that broke a purchased
database, and data-localisation rules are satisfied by running in AWS
Mumbai. The pressure that manufactures foundational software was never
applied.

There is the **jugaad trap**. Indian engineering venerates the frugal
workaround assembled from existing parts,[^16] which is admirable at the
product layer and the exact opposite of the foundational impulse: jugaad
celebrates the existing foundation and builds cleverly atop it, where
SQLite and OceanBase exist because someone judged the foundation wrong
and rebuilt it. A culture that rewards the workaround over the rebuild
produces applications. It is doing precisely what it is optimised to do.

There is the **silo, and the price of failure**. The domain-expert
mechanism needs a doctor or a geographer to be able to learn to code ---
and the system to tolerate the time. Indian professional education is
unusually siloed, and the failure cost is asymmetric: elsewhere a
researcher who spends two years on a tool that fails loses two years;
here they can lose their standing, funding, and career. The mechanism
that produced SPM requires permission to fail at the primary task, and
the system declines to grant it.

And there is the **feedback loop that never closed** --- the most
instructive of the four, because it shows the missing ingredient is not
what people assume.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">BARC did the documentation right, and it still wasn't enough</div>
<p>
The usual story is that India's state computing failed for want of documentation. It
is more uncomfortable than that. India's Department of Atomic Energy, through BARC,
built more than twenty ANUPAM supercomputers from 1991 onward and documented them
properly — in peer-reviewed papers, in Springer volumes, in the scientific
record.[fn:barc] C-DAC's PARAM, by contrast, lives mostly in press releases. Yet
neither produced reusable open software, and BARC's good documentation did not save
it, because ANUPAM was built for <em>internal</em> reactor physics — there was no
external user to pull the work outward, fork it, demand it improve. The missing
ingredient was never the blog. It was the downstream user.
</p>
</div>

The same lesson, generalised, separates India\'s state programs from the
comparators that worked.

  Country       Body           What it built                     Did it yield reusable software?
  ------------- -------------- --------------------------------- ------------------------------------------
  India         C-DAC, C-DOT   Supercomputers, exchanges         Hardware yes; reusable OSS no
  India         BARC (DAE)     20+ ANUPAM supercomputers         Documented science; internal-use; no OSS
  Brazil        Embrapa        Agricultural research, networks   Yes --- tied to farmer problems
  South Korea   ETRI           Telecoms, chips, accelerators     Yes --- handed to industry
  Taiwan        NCHC / ITRI    HPC coupled to semiconductors     Yes --- feeds the chip ecosystem

India built machines. The comparators built capabilities coupled to a
user who exerted pressure. C-DAC also lost on procurement, where
specifications were quietly written to match the incumbent foreign
vendor, and on retention, where the services boom tripled salaries and
the capability walked out, leaving process behind. OceanBase improved
because Alipay\'s load broke it in public; ANUPAM and PARAM faced no
such pressure and calcified.

And when the talent did build foundational software, it often did so
elsewhere, for someone else.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Apigee — the export, named</div>
<p>
The API-management company that became Apigee began as Sonoa Systems in 2004 in Santa
Clara, founded by Raj Singh — an engineer of Indian origin, from Meerut — and Ravi
Chandra; Chet Kapoor joined as CEO in 2006 and later took it public. Google bought it
in 2016 for about 625 million dollars.[fn:apigee] An engineer with an Indian name built
foundational API infrastructure that twenty of the Fortune 100 came to depend on. He
built it in San Jose, on American capital, for an American acquirer. The talent was not
absent. The system that would have kept it in India was. This is the most concrete
instance of the talent being exported at a discount.
</p>
</div>

The composite is fairly called a pursuit of the average --- not an
insult to individuals, many of them world-class, but a description of
what the system selects for. The safe path is the services job, the
application on someone else\'s cloud, the workaround admired in the
standup. The foundational build --- slow, uncertain, demanding both a
tolerance for failure and a serene indifference to the premium career
--- is exactly the profile the incentive structure filters out before
the interview.

# The Transition Is Real --- and Going the Wrong Way {#transition}

Here the story turns, because something genuinely new is happening. The
Indian companies that survived and scaled are now descending into the
foundational layer --- building the floor because the borrowed one
finally stopped fitting. And one company went there on principle, before
it had to.

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Zerodha — the model already working</div>
<p>
A thirty-person tech team runs roughly a fifth of India's stock trades, about fourteen
million trades on a record day, at sub-forty-millisecond mean latency, on self-hosted
open source with no vendor lock-in.[fn:zerodha-s] They open-source what they build and
run it themselves — DungBeetle, a distributed SQL job server; kaf-relay, for Kafka
replication; Listmonk — and they <em>co-founded FOSS United</em> and put a million
dollars a year into a FOSS fund.[fn:zerodha-o] Their own blog states the disposition
plainly: "Nobody has any background in finance… everyone has been a hacker or a
hobbyist straight out of college. What colleges, neither do we ask, nor do we keep a
track of."[fn:zerodha-blog] When a four-decade, single-handed Kannada dictionary
surfaced, it was discovered and open-sourced not by a university but by this
brokerage. It is the iconoclast and the domain expert and the connective tissue, in
one company.
</p>
</div>

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Drove — built, opened, and waiting for an audience</div>
<p>
PhonePe — a payments company — built its own container orchestrator, Drove, as a
declared successor to Apache Mesos/Marathon, and a bare-metal provisioner, Nimbus, on
custom iPXE.[fn:drove] Drove is genuinely open-sourced, documented, and was presented
for technical review at Rootconf in early 2025. It is real, it runs PhonePe's
production infrastructure — and it is almost entirely un-adopted outside PhonePe. There
is no chorus of outside companies, no "we switched from Kubernetes to Drove" write-ups.
This is the new failure mode: not built-but-not-opened, but built, opened, and left at
the edge of adoption for want of the unglamorous evangelism — the benchmarks, the
outsider documentation, the marketing — that turns a published repository into a
standard.
</p>
</div>

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Frappe — the unglamorous birth</div>
<p>
India's one genuine application-framework export began without ceremony. Frappe's
ERPNext was licensed under the GPL and sat on Google Code from 2009; its founder
rewrote it, by his own account, "a couple of times," and in the early days it had "no
clear deployment instructions, very sparse documentation and no community
forum."[fn:frappe] From that unpromising start it grew a forum now past twenty-two
thousand members. Foundational-ish software, born ugly, kept alive by persistence.
</p>
</div>

<div class="story-box">
<div class="story-label">Zoho — the descent made on principle</div>
<p>
Zoho is the outlier that matters most. Founded in 1996 as AdventNet by Sridhar Vembu —
IIT Madras, a Princeton PhD, ex-Qualcomm, who returned not to Bangalore but to the
town of Tenkasi in rural Tamil Nadu — it bootstrapped to over a hundred million users
and well past a billion dollars in revenue without ever taking venture capital, and is
still private.[fn:zoho-sv] Its first product, in the 1990s, was WebNMS, a
network-management framework sold to telcos worldwide — infrastructure, not an
application, from the very start.[fn:webnms] It built its own scripting language,
Deluge, to run Zoho Creator and Flow; an owned language is an owned layer. It shipped
Ulaa, a Chromium-based browser whose DNS routes through India's national root
servers.[fn:ulaa] And, most strikingly, Zoho does not rent its foundation: its products
run on hardware Zoho owns and software frameworks Zoho wrote, atop Linux and Postgres,
in its own data centres — not on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud — and in 2026 it unveiled
Nathu La, a server motherboard and chassis it designed in-house, for sovereignty and to
cut inference cost.[fn:zoho-server]
</p>
<p>
Every other company in this story descended into the foundations because scale forced
it. Zoho descended because Vembu decided it was the right thing to do — before the
scale arrived, before the database broke, before anyone called it necessary. Zoho is
not open source, and the thesis holds regardless: the issue is foundational work, not
licensing. Zoho is the proof that the decision to own the floor can be made on
principle, years before economics demands it.
</p>
</div>

This descent is real, recent, and the right direction. But for the most
part it is a privilege of the already-scaled: it flows downward, only
after winning at scale, and each winner builds only the few pieces its
own problem demands. That is a structural ceiling. Few companies reach
that scale, so the country gets few foundational projects, each narrow.

The encouraging exception to the ceiling is FOSS United --- co-founded
by Zerodha --- which is the closest thing India has to the connective
tissue this essay keeps naming as missing: a network, conferences, a
fund, the beginnings of a place where a builder in one town can discover
the builder in another who solved the same problem last year. It is the
right bridge, half-built.

What India still does not have is the other half: the garage aimed at a
genuinely big problem before scale forces it. The lone builder normally
has a small problem an existing library handles, so jugaad is rational
and they stay at the top of the stack. The SQLite phenotype --- the
small builder going deep before anyone has said it is worth money --- is
precisely the thing the descent of the scaled winners cannot supply, and
it is the thing the country most lacks.

# A Way Out That Does Not Run Through the IITs {#path}

The reflexive prescription points at the IITs. It is almost certainly
wrong: their graduates have the best options in the country, and the
student who builds a distributed database is declining a comfortable
offer to do something hard with an uncertain payoff. The elite
institution has the talent and exactly the wrong incentives.

A better target is the engineer at a B-tier polytechnic in a tier-two
city --- strong fundamentals, fewer escape routes, and proximity to a
real problem no existing software solves. Picture a garment cluster like
Tiruppur, or a mining-transport hub. A local transporter --- a dozen
trucks, family-run --- has a routing problem whose addresses mix Tamil
script, transliteration, and informal landmarks that Google Maps treats
with contempt. No enterprise software addresses it at a price the
transporter can pay, because the market is too small to interest anyone
in California. The nearby polytechnic has students who need projects.
Every part of the forcing function is already in the room: a real
problem, no budget, local capability, and an economics that makes
proprietary software impossible --- so the output is open by default,
not from virtue but because there is nobody to sell it to.

Kerala already ran this experiment by accident: a budget-driven decision
to put open-source software in schools produced, two decades on, a
generation of foundational contributors. No grand vision was required,
and none was present.

And one company has been running the talent half of this experiment on
purpose for two decades. Zoho hires without degrees, from tier-two and
tier-three towns, and trains its people internally through Zoho Schools;
its headquarters is a village, and the village is treated as the asset
rather than the handicap. The experiment this essay asks someone to run
--- strong local talent, a real problem, freedom from the premium-
career pull --- is the experiment Zoho has been quietly running since
the mid-2000s, and it is no coincidence that the same company owns its
stack from the server motherboard to the browser.

Alongside the organic, a directed programme on the DARPA and 863 pattern
--- not research grants, which buy papers, but engineering contracts,
which buy software, with named deliverables and dates. Ten named
problems: a distributed database tuned for UPI-scale transaction graphs,
a production-grade Indian-language morphological analyser, an open
pipeline turning ISRO imagery into per-field guidance for a farmer, a
genomics toolkit calibrated for Indian population structure rather than
borrowed European references.[^17] Run them at regional institutions,
because the constraint is the asset.

And the two bridges, stated plainly, because they are the whole fix: a
forcing function for the garage, so the small builder meets a big
problem before scale would otherwise force it; and a
marketing-and-adoption layer for the descenders, so that a Drove becomes
a shared foundation rather than a private one. The pressures that might
supply the first are already assembling on their own --- the
data-localisation provisions of the DPDP Act, the RBI\'s circulars on
cloud concentration, the public compute of the IndiaAI Mission that
produced Sarvam. Whether anyone names them and points them at the
infrastructure layers is a choice, and it has not yet been made.

# Early Signs, and an Honest Close {#close}

India writes a staggering quantity of software and is not, with a thin
and widening set of exceptions, a foundational software nation. China
is. The gap appears the moment one stops counting developers and starts
counting databases and message queues, and it is not a gap of talent ---
the engineers who built much of the world\'s foundational infrastructure
trained here and built it for American companies, on American payrolls.
Apigee is the named, specific exemplar: the talent was exported at a
discount.

But the close is not despair, because the early signs are real. The
scaled winners are descending into the foundations --- Zerodha, PhonePe,
Frappe --- and Zoho went there on principle before scale forced it.
Zerodha is building the connective tissue the country lacked. Sarvam has
done something genuinely open, even if it is a model and not yet a
floor. The descent has begun. What has not begun is the ascent --- the
garage going deep before scale forces it --- and that is the half that
produces the SQLites, because the SQLite phenotype is by definition the
small builder attacking a deep problem before anyone has told them it is
worth money.

The dedication in SQLite\'s source is not a policy instrument. *Share
freely, never taking more than you give* is a disposition, and
dispositions cannot be issued as an order. But the conditions under
which that disposition turns into software can be deliberately built,
and India has not built them at scale. That is a choice --- and, unlike
talent, it is one the country still gets to make, while the window is
open and Sarvam is still the exception rather than the proof.

# Further Areas of Interest {#further}

This essay opens more threads than one piece can pursue. Each of the
following is hinted at above and deserves its own treatment.

-   **The licence as geopolitics.** Apache-2.0 versus MulanPubL-2.0 was
    treated here as a signal of intent; a fuller study would map how
    licence choice tracks the ambition to be adopted globally versus the
    obligation to comply domestically --- and what that implies for a
    Western enterprise\'s legal review of any China-origin tool.

-   **The Apache Foundation as trust machinery.** Fourteen Chinese
    projects routed through the ASF; MOSIP, India\'s cleanest export,
    did not. Why incubation at a neutral foundation functions as an
    internationalisation step, and why Indian projects skip it, is a
    question this essay only gestures at.

-   **The Sri Lanka comparison, properly.** WSO2 and Siddhi suggest a
    22-million-person country ran the cross-disciplinary mechanism India
    did not. What did the University of Moratuwa do --- institutionally
    --- that Indian engineering colleges have not, and is it
    transferable?

-   **The model layer\'s trajectory.** Sarvam is, for now, an open model
    rather than an open foundation. Whether the IndiaAI cohort (Sarvam,
    BharatGen, Soket AI, and others) descends into the infrastructure
    beneath the models --- training stacks, serving engines, inference
    runtimes --- is the single most important thing to watch over the
    next three years.

-   **Zoho as a controlled experiment.** A company that owns its stack
    from an in-house server motherboard to a browser, hires from
    villages without degrees, and refuses outside capital is a natural
    experiment in nearly every prescription this essay makes. Its
    successes and its limits both deserve close, unsentimental study ---
    including where vertical integration becomes a constraint rather
    than a strength.

-   **The marketing-and-adoption gap.** Drove is built, opened, and
    largely un-adopted. The unglamorous discipline that turns a
    published repository into a standard --- benchmarks, outsider
    documentation, conference advocacy, semantic-versioning discipline
    --- is itself a capability India underinvests in, and it would repay
    a dedicated study of what successful open-source go-to-market
    actually requires.

-   **The protocol-to-code question.** ONDC ships reference
    implementations; Zoho is in talks with iSpirt to publish an open
    messaging protocol for Arattai. Whether India\'s genuine strength at
    the protocol layer can be deliberately converted into deployable
    foundational code is perhaps the most actionable open question of
    all.

-   **The returning diaspora.** Apigee\'s foundational work happened in
    San Jose. With visa friction rising and remote work normalised, some
    of that talent is returning or building from India. Whether the
    system can now retain what it once exported is a live, testable
    proposition.

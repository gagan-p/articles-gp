#+begin_quote
*Methodology Disclaimer*

This essay began with a /pre-existing hypothesis/: that India does not produce foundational systems software. I subsequently sought data to support this thesis. Where data was ambiguous, I erred toward the hypothesis — a form of *force-fitting*.

The quantitative claims are sourced to the footnotes. However, the /interpretive architecture/ — the “mad guy,” “institutional incapacity,” and “organisational myopia” sections — are *analytical provocations and literary devices*, not empirically verified facts. The mechanism taxonomy (§7) is a theoretical model, not a peer-reviewed framework. Corrections and counter-evidence are explicitly welcome.
#+end_quote

* 1. The Claim

** 1.1 The Differentiation in Software Types

India is called a software nation. That title conflates two distinct layers that the industry itself keeps separate.

| Layer | What it is | Examples |
|-------+-----------+----------|
| Application | What a user actually touches | wallets, dashboards, ERP, SaaS |
| Middleware | Glue between apps and the floor | app frameworks, API gateways, ORMs |
| Systems / foundational | The floor everything runs on | OS, database, message broker, runtime |

Foundational software (systems software) is the floor. The litmus test is simple: *does other software depend on it?* Whoever owns the floor sets the terms. India's headline numbers all measure the top row. *India is a tenant.*

** 1.2 India's Success: Listed Giants, All in the Application Layer

*** 1.2.1 Large listed entities, large employments, Business model ==> cost arbitrage off shoring

These are not technology companies in the Silicon Valley sense. They are labour‑cost arbitrage firms that happen to use code.

| Company | Revenue (FY25–26, approx) | Employees | Business Model |
|---------+------------------------+-----------+----------------|
| TCS | US$30B[fn:23] | 584,519+[fn:14] | IT services, offshoring |
| Infosys | US$19.5B[fn:24] | ~323,788[fn:14] | IT services, offshoring |
| Wipro | US$10.4B[fn:25] | ~233,232[fn:14] | IT services, offshoring |
| HCL Tech | US$13B+[fn:26] | ~223,000[fn:14] | IT services, offshoring |
| Tech Mahindra | US$6B+[fn:27] | ~148,000[fn:14] | IT services, offshoring |

The model is straightforward: Indian engineers at a fraction of Western cost. It is *not* technology arbitrage. Revenue per employee has hovered around *US$50,000* for the sector over FY2020–FY2024[fn:28].

*** 1.2.2 Patents, Products, and FOSS

- *Patents*: India's IT services giants hold far fewer software patents than their Western counterparts (Google: ~50,000; Microsoft: ~60,000; TCS: ~2,000). Most patents are in process/methodology, not core technology.[fn:1]
- *Products*: Few globally adopted software products originate from India. Tally, Freshworks, and Zoho are exceptions — and all sit at the application layer.
- *FOSS contributions*: Indian companies have minimal corporate presence in the Apache Foundation, CNCF, or the Linux kernel. What contributions exist come from individuals, most often employed by American firms.

#+begin_quote
India's software success is real — but it is application software and services, built on a floor owned elsewhere. The business model is arbitrage, not invention.
#+end_quote

* 2. The Counter-Claim: Is It a Talent Problem?

** 2.1 Volume Is Not DepthIndia hosts roughly *17 million developers on GitHub* — a large share of the global base.[fn:2] But ownership is measured by who *commits* to foundational open source, not who *uses* it.

| Country | Developer Share (approx) | Foundational OSS Contribution (author estimate) |
|---------+----------------------+-----------------------------------------------|
| USA | ~18% | ~35% |
| China | ~16% | ~18% |
| India | ~17% | ~2% |
| UK | ~6% | ~6% |
| Germany | ~5% | ~5% |

The 2% figure for India is an author estimate based on Apache Foundation geographic patterns and GitHub Innovation Graph active‑user data[fn:29]; it is not a primary survey finding.

~17 million developers producing no load‑bearing database, kernel, or broker is not latent talent — it is a surplus of the average. *Volume, not depth.*

* 7. The Five Mechanisms

** 7.1 Definitions

Five mechanisms recur across foundational projects. This taxonomy is a theoretical model; it is not peer‑reviewed.

| # | Mechanism | Description | Examples |
|---+-----------+-------------+----------|
| 1 | Lone obsessive | One person rebuilds a tool judged wrong at root | SQLite, Git, Gitea |
| 5 | Forcing function | Scale problem refuses workaround | OceanBase, Spanner |
| 3 | Target‑bound backing | Commissioning agency with a real user | DARPA, 863 Programme |
| 2 | Mass community | Many hands, shared standard, no single owner | Linux, PostgreSQL, K8s |
| 4 | Cross‑domain carry | Domain expert builds because no tool exists | CERN (Web), R |

**Theory**: Foundational software is built through these five mechanisms. Two drivers dominate: *passion* (1) and/or *need* (5).

Distribution of "The 15" by mechanism:

| Mechanism | Count | Share (of 8 max) |
|-----------+-------+------------------|
| Lone obsessive | 7 |87.5% |
| Forcing function | 6 | 75% |
| Target‑bound backing | 6 | 75% |
| Mass community | 5 | 62.5% |
| Cross‑domain carry | 2 | 25% |
| Elite teaching institution | 0 | 0% |
| State, for captive user | 0 | 0% |

*Note: One project can fire multiple mechanisms, hence the single-project denominator is 8 (max count).*

* 8. Case Studies

#+begin_quote
**Frappe**

- *What*: Application framework (like Django/Rails); ERPNext built on it[fn:9]
- *Origin*: 2009, GPL on Google Code
- *How*: Lone builder, no adequate open‑source ERP for SMBs
- *Mechanism*: **1** — lone obsessive
- *Scale*: Tens of thousands in community; thousands of organisations
- *Status*: Active, maintained, growing
- *Significance*: The only Indian framework of consequence — built because no adequate tool existed
#+end_quote

#+begin_quote
**PhonePe / Drove**

- *What*: Container orchestrator + bare‑metal provisioner (Drove)
- *Origin*: PhonePe internal; open‑sourced on GitHub
- *How*: PhonePe's scale (~350K cores, 3PB) broke existing tools; they built their own[fn:4]
- *Mechanism*: **5 + 3** — scale required it; open‑sourced to share
- *Scale*: ~350K cores, 3PB, ~7s VM creation; PPeC agent manages bare‑metal fleet
- *Status*: Built, open‑sourced, largely un‑adopted outside PhonePe
- *Significance*: Proves Indian firms at scale can build the floor; also proves building is not enough — mechanism 2 (community) is missing
#+end_quote

#+begin_quote
**Zerodha**

- *What*: Brokerage + open‑source funding body + infrastructure tools[fn:10]
- *Origin*: Zerodha (2010); FLOSS/fund (Oct 2024)
- *Tools built*:
  - DungBeetle: distributed SQL job server
  - kaf‑relay: Kafka replication tool
  - Listmonk: email list manager (AGPL, used globally)
- *FLOSS/fund*: US$1M/year to global open source (first round US$100K to OpenSSL)
- *FOSS United*: Co‑founded to run FOSS conferences and city chapters
- *Philosophy*: "Nobody has any background in finance… everyone has been a hacker or a hobbyist straight out of college. What colleges, neither do we ask, nor do we keep track of."
- *Mechanism*: **2** — private firm seeding the commons
- *Scale*: Runs ~20% of India's trades (~14M/day), sub‑40ms latency, self‑hosted open source
- *Significance*: A private firm building mechanism 2 because it chose to, not because a balance sheet demanded it
#+end_quote

#+begin_quote
**MOSIP**

- *What*: Modular Open Source Identity Platform — digital ID system[fn:3]
- *Origin*: IIIT‑Bangalore, from 2018
- *How*: Real government user needing an identity system; built as a reference implementation of Aadhaar principles
- *Mechanism*: **5** — real external user pulling the software into the open
- *Scale*: ~29 countries, ~185M+ IDs generated by 2026
- *Caveat*: Runs on AWS — even this export sits on foreign infrastructure
- *Significance*: Proof that India can build globally adopted foundational software when a real forcing function exists
#+end_quote

* 9. Why Have We Failed?

#+begin_quote
*Interpretive Framework Ahead*

The subsections that follow — "mad guys," "support for madness," and "organisational myopia" — are *analytical devices and literary provocations*. They are not derived from survey data, econometric models, or institutional ethnography. They are presented to test the hypothesis, not to assert it as fact.
#+end_quote

** 9.1 Do We Not Have Enough Mad Guys?

*Assumption.* We have them. They are simply not building foundations. The incentive structure pulls every "mad" engineer toward the next fintech app, the next SaaS product, the next services contract — the application layer, where the money is.

** 9.2 Do We Not Support Those Who Want to Be Mad?

*Assumption.* No. The support systems are missing:

- No patient capital for foundational work.
- No institutional backing for "build a database" projects.
- No neutral governance (like Apache) for Indian-origin projects.
- No community machinery to sustain a project after the initial commit.

The rare successes — Zerodha, MOSIP — happened *despite* the system, not because of it.

** 9.3 Do Our Organisations Look Only at the Immediate?

*Assumption.* Yes. The Indian IT industry lives on quarterly billing cycles, not decadal horizons.

- TCS / Infosys / Wipro: billable hours, not R&D[fn:28].
- Startups: VC pressure for growth velocity, not engineering depth.
- Government: five‑year plans, not twenty‑year technology roadmaps.

China's 863 Programme (1986) funded projects with 10–15 year horizons. India's STPI (1991) had a similar horizon — but pointed it at services export, not foundations.

** 9.4 What Our Governments Are Doing

*** 9.4.1 China's 863 & Other Programmes vs India's Incentives

*China's model*:
- *863 Programme (1986)*: Outcome‑based funding, named deliverables, real users[fn:5]
- *Torch Programme (1988)*: Commercialisation bridge — lab to industry
- *IOE replacement mandate*: Remove IBM / Oracle / EMC — forced substitution
- *Result*: OceanBase, openEuler, RocketMQ

*India's model*:
- *STPI (1991)*: Tax breaks, bandwidth, parks — all pointed at services export
- *BOSS (2000s)*: A Linux re‑spin with no adoption and no feedback loop
- *CDAC / PARAM*: Built machines, not reusable software
- *Current incentives*: YouTube videos, hackathons, "innovation" grants that fund demos, not production software

The contrast is not subtle.

Further reading: [Orange Economy Critique](https://gagan-p.github.io/articles-gp/articles/orange-economy-critique.html)

* 10. What Can We Do?

** 10.1 Scrap the Elite-Industry-Government Theory

Can we scrap the theory that elite institutions + big corporates + government backing will bring fundamental change?

- *Mechanism 1 (lone obsessive)*: Elite institutions optimise for escape velocity, not obsession. The graduate who could build a database leaves — abroad or to the app layer.
- *Mechanism 2 (mass community)*: Elite institutions are small and insular. They do not produce the distributed communities that build Linux or Kubernetes.
- *Mechanism 3 (target‑bound backing)*: Funding is grant‑based, not outcome‑based. No DARPA‑style "deliver this capability" culture exists.
- *Mechanism 4 (cross‑domain carry)*: Silos prevent the scientist‑programmer from emerging. A doctor cannot work with a computer scientist.
- *Mechanism 5 (intolerant requirement)*: No production forcing function. Students do not face a Singles' Day problem.

#+begin_quote
Conclusion: The elite model is aligned against four of the five mechanisms. Asking it to produce foundations is asking it to do what it is structurally incapable of doing.
#+end_quote

** 10.2 Propose: Small Clusters + Local Institutes

*The alternative*: clusters of small firms with a real, shared, unsolved problem and no budget to evade it.

*Elite‑Institution Model* → against mechanisms 1, 2, 4, 5

*Cluster + Polytechnic Model* → instantiates mechanisms 1, 2, 4, 5:
- Real problem, no budget = forcing function (mech 5)
- Local builders, not on an escape trajectory (mech 1)
- Firms are domain experts, students are computing carry (mech 4)
- No market to sell to, so open by default (mech 2)

**Concrete clusters**:

| Cluster | Problem | What a solution would be |
|---------+---------+--------------------------|
| Garment (Tiruppur) | Logistics with mixed‑script addresses | Route optimisation with Tamil/English address parsing |
| Mining‑transport hub | Truck scheduling, load tracking | Simple NoSQL DB with a text interface for data entry |
| Agriculture (mandi) | Price discovery, quality grading | Mobile‑first DB with offline sync |
| Small‑town retail | Inventory, billing, credit | Lightweight ERP with Indian language UI |

*Kerala's accident*: FOSS in schools → Malayalam computing community (fonts, transliteration, morphology tools). The forcing function was poverty, not policy.

* Close

India's software success is real, and it is the success of a *tenant*. Forty years of services and arbitrage produced wealth and reputation — and not the capacity to build the floor.

China proves the second set can be assembled. The fifteen cases show what assembles it. The handful of Indian stories show how rarely the same engine fires at home.

India does not lack people — it has supplied the world's foundational software with its people for decades. It lacks the *conditions* under which those people do that work *at home*. Jugaad is the antonym of the disposition that builds floors. The institution it trusts most is the least suited to the task. The capacity is a choice about conditions, not a fact about talent — and the choice is still open.

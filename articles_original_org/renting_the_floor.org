* India's Software Industry Is a Success
:PROPERTIES:

The Indian software industry is world-class: US$283bn[fn:nasscom] in annual
revenue, 5.1% growth[fn:nasscom] in a weak global market, 5.80mn people
employed,[fn:nasscom] and the largest and fastest-growing base of developers in the

* India Sells Services and Applications, Not Products It Owns
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: value-chain
:END:

The scale of §1 sits in the low-capture part of the chain: India writes and runs
software that others own, priced below what those owners would pay at home. The
value captured is the cost gap, not the software.

*** What Is Excluded, and Why
:PROPERTIES:

:END:

The test is whether a firm sells software, not whether it is built on software. By
IDC's definition the software market is revenue from licensing or subscribing software
to other organisations.[fn:idc] The firms below build sophisticated software but
monetise a service operated /on/ it — advertising, retail, payments, transport — so
their revenue is not software revenue and they sit outside the software market.

| Naver | search / portal | advertising, commerce | internet services[fn:naver] |

Including them would conflate /runs on software/ with /sells software/ — the exact
distinction this essay draws.

*** Messaging: the Same Split, One Layer Down
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: messaging
:END:

Consumer messaging is not a software-vendor category — these firms monetise ads,
payments, or content, not licences. But it is the clearest test of the foundational
question, because building a messenger can yield a /protocol/ the world adopts, or
merely an app at scale.

| App | Origin | Innovation, and what layer it sits at |
|-----+--------+---------------------------------------|
| Signal | US | The Signal Protocol (Double Ratchet + X3DH key exchange) — the end-to-end-encryption standard the industry adopted[fn:signal] |
| Telegram | Russia | MTProto — its own transport-and-crypto protocol, built below the app; MTProto 2.0 formally verified[fn:mtproto] |
| WhatsApp | US (Meta) | Not a new layer: built on open, peer-reviewed foundations — Erlang/BEAM, Mnesia, FreeBSD, an ejabberd-derived XMPP server — with encryption licensed from the Signal Protocol[fn:whatsapp] |
| WeChat | China | Super-app ecosystem (payments, mini-programs) at national scale; proprietary, no open protocol [Inferred] |
| LINE | Japan | App-layer; "Letter Sealing" end-to-end on standard primitives [Inferred] |
| KakaoTalk | Korea | App-layer; near-universal domestic reach, no protocol contribution [Inferred] |
| Hike | India | App-layer (stickers, local languages); reached unicorn status, shut down its messenger in 2021[fn:hike] |
| Koo | India | App-layer (X/Twitter alternative); shut down 2024[fn:koo] |
| Arattai (Zoho) | India | App-layer, domestically hosted (no AWS/Azure); government-endorsed 100× surge in Sept 2025, lost ranking by October; E2E added reactively in Nov 2025 on a conventional scheme, not a new protocol[fn:arattai] |
| JioChat | India | App-layer; never reached scale [Inferred] |

[Author judgement] The pattern across these rows is a claim about /why/ consumer
software gets adopted at scale. Adoption is earned one of two ways: by building
something fundamentally new that others must then build on — a protocol — or by great
engineering assembled on great, open foundations. Signal took the first path: the
Signal Protocol is a genuine primitive, and WhatsApp, Google RCS, and others encrypt
with it rather than reinvent it. WhatsApp took the second: it poured none of its own
floor, but it stood on the best open, peer-reviewed foundations available — Erlang and
its BEAM runtime and Mnesia database (Ericsson/OTP), FreeBSD, an ejabberd-derived XMPP
server, the Signal Protocol — and engineered brilliantly on top. Telegram took the
first path more aggressively, building MTProto, its own transport-and-crypto stack;
it is contested precisely because it rolled its own rather than compose vetted parts.

The common thread is exposure, not secrecy. A protocol succeeds only by publication
and peer review; great engineering on open foundations succeeds only by improving
software a competitor can inspect and fork. Durable consumer software is backed either
by a fundamental technical change or by engineering on an open floor — and in both
cases the work is out in the open, unafraid of scrutiny or of being improved upon.

[Author judgement] India is absent from both routes. It produced neither a protocol
nor an app engineered to last on those foundations; its entrants reached the app layer,
added nothing back to the floor they consumed, and folded.

[Author judgement] A captive home market is the forcing function that builds durable
products, and eventually the floor beneath them (§7). India sold outward and never
hit that wall.

** The Honest Exceptions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: exceptions
:END:

India has produced real foundational-adjacent software. It clusters in three shapes —
the lone individual, the re-domiciled firm, the internal tool — none of which becomes a
systems primitive the world depends on.

| Project | What it is | Where it sits | The catch |
|---------+------------+---------------+-----------|
| Calibre[fn:calibre] | e-book manager, ~500k LOC | application | one person, ~18 years, unaffiliated |
| Kitty[fn:kitty] | GPU terminal emulator | systems-tier | same person; see below |
| Hasura[fn:hasura] | GraphQL query engine over Postgres, in Haskell | foundational-adjacent | re-domiciled to the US; see below |
| kaf-relay[fn:zerodha] | Kafka-to-Kafka replication tool | glue on Kafka | one firm's internal tool, open-sourced |
| DungBeetle[fn:dungbeetle] | SQL job server / async reporting | glue on Postgres | one firm's internal tool, open-sourced |

[Author judgement] Zerodha open-sources the infrastructure it builds for itself —

The reflex at the wall is the tell. When Zepto outgrew its database it
switched vendors (Aurora → DynamoDB → MongoDB) and filed patches upstream;[fn:zepto]
when Alibaba outgrew Oracle it wrote OceanBase (§7). Same capability, opposite
response — nothing forced the Indian firm to build the primitive, and its economics
argued for renting it.

** Contribution Does Not Scale With Headcount
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: contribution
:END:

India has the largest developer base on earth (§1). Its contribution to shared code
does not track that headcount.

| Contributions to EU-based open-source projects (2025) | Count |
|-------------------------------------------------------+-------|
| United States | 934k+ |
| United Kingdom | 553k+ |
| India | 347k+ |

Source: European Commission OSS study, GitHub Innovation Graph data.[fn:eugit]

India — with several times the UK's developer count — contributes less in absolute
terms. Two structural facts explain the gap. Contribution to foundational projects
is tracked by /employer/, not nationality: the Linux Foundation ranks Intel, Google,

Gitee (§7).[fn:eugit] At the country level, the one peer-reviewed measure puts India
at ~5.4% of active open-source contributors — below the level its income
predicts.[fn:wachs]

* What Software Actually Is
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: layers
:END:

| Layer | What it is | Examples |
|-------+------------+----------|
| Application | what a person uses | wallets, dashboards, ERP, office suites |
| Middleware | glue between apps and the floor | frameworks, API gateways, query engines |
| Foundational (systems) | the floor everything runs on | OS, database, message broker, runtime, cryptography |

The trade term for the bottom row is /systems software/. Other software depends on it,
and when it fails, everything above it fails at once. Value follows that dependency:
whoever owns the floor sets the price, the roadmap, the licence, and the shutdown date
for everyone standing on it. A nation strong only in the top row is a tenant, however
prosperous.

* Same Start, Different Choice
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: history
:END:

A generation ago China stood where India stands now: assembling and servicing what
others designed. Around 2000, ranked on software, India was ahead — English, the
services contracts, the export head-start. What changed was not the starting line but
the direction each pointed its industry. India deepened services. China built the
layer beneath. India's own attempts at a floor show the difference was direction, not
capacity.

Kerala put free software in its schools from the mid-2000s (IT@School, later KITE),
because it could not afford licences. Two decades on, the state produces a
disproportionate share of India's Malayalam-language and free-software contributors —
fonts, morphology, localisation. Poverty was the forcing function. It usually is.
</p>
</div>
#+end_export

* China Built the Floor
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: china
:END:

China's move into foundational software was driven by strategy and siege: the "delete
IOE" policy (remove IBM servers, Oracle databases, EMC storage), export controls, and
consumer platforms at a scale purchased Western software could not survive.

#+begin_export html
<div class="story">
<div class="story-label">The story of OceanBase</div>
<p>
In 2010 Alipay ran on Oracle, and on Singles' Day — the largest shopping
event on earth — the load exceeded what any purchased database was built to survive.
Ant Group decided the database itself was wrong for the problem and rebuilt it, in
C++, from the basics, over a decade.
</p>
<p>
It replaced Oracle in production gradually — ten percent of Alipay's traffic, then
all of it. In October 2019 OceanBase recorded 60.88 million tpmC on the TPC-C
benchmark, taking the world record off Oracle's 2010 mark of 30.25 million; in May
2020 it reached 707 million, peer-reviewed at VLDB 2022.
A firm that rented Oracle a decade earlier held the record Oracle used to own — and
by 2025 Alibaba Cloud's PolarDB pushed it to 2.055 billion.
</p>
<p>
The lesson is not that Chinese engineers are gifted. A real wall — a load that could
not be bought around, in front of a firm that could not walk away — produced a
foundation. Even here one Western brick remains: OceanBase's stored-procedure compiler
is built on LLVM. Nobody pours every floor; the point is China pours most of it.
</p>
</div>
#+end_export

| Layer | Ant built | Because |
|-------+-----------+---------|
| Database | OceanBase | Oracle could not survive Singles' Day |
| Messaging | RocketMQ | the alternatives lacked Taobao's guarantees |
| Service mesh | MOSN | Envoy could not carry Ant's protocols |
| Consensus | SOFAJRaft | latency, at a scale that punishes it |
| Registry | Nacos | to replace Consul, etcd, and ZooKeeper at once |

Source: OceanBase VLDB paper and project repositories.[fn:oceanbase][fn:tpcc]

Beyond Ant, the same pattern repeats across firms:

- ByteDance built CloudWeGo (Go and Rust microservice frameworks) for TikTok-scale traffic.
- Huawei shipped openEuler (a server OS) and openGauss (a database) when RHEL became a strategic risk.
- PingCAP built TiDB, a distributed SQL database that implements the design in Google's Spanner paper as open source.

Between 2015 and 2024, fourteen Chinese-origin infrastructure projects were donated to
the Apache Software Foundation, and now run in production worldwide.[fn:apache]

#+begin_export html
<div class="story">
<div class="story-label">The story of Gitee</div>
<p>
Not all of it came from giants under siege. Gitee began as a domestic Git-hosting
forge and became China's answer to GitHub — today the state-backed default where a
large share of Chinese open source lives, which is precisely why GitHub-based
measures undercount China (§3). China did not only build its floor through
state-mandated champions; it also repatriated the developer infrastructure itself.
</p>
</div>
#+end_export

* Products vs Services, and Who Contributes the Floor
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: products-vs-services
:END:

At the platform layer — the software that runs private clouds and telecom cores — the
difference is not capability but posture: China contributes to the source; India
ships or buys the binaries.

| Platform-software case | China | India |
|------------------------+-------+-------|
| Private cloud (OpenStack) | 99Cloud: top-10 global OpenStack contributor[fn:99cloud] | StackUp: OpenStack-based product; little upstream; few large deployments |
| Telecom core (NFV / orchestration) | China Telecom: OpenStack-Powered cloud; co-founded Open-O → ONAP[fn:chinatelecom] | BSNL Bharat stack: indigenous 4G (C-DOT, Tejas, TCS), but closed and SI-delivered[fn:bsnl] |

Both countries built a sovereign telecom stack — so the difference is isolated to
openness. China Telecom turned its telco-cloud work into an upstream standard
(Open-O merged into ONAP); India's Bharat stack is a closed national-champion project
promoted for export.[fn:bsnl] Indian banks, meanwhile, consume open source only
through Red Hat's commercial distributions, and are publicly branded for "open-source
excellence" while owning none of it.[fn:redhatbanks]

#+begin_export html
<div class="story">
<div class="story-label">Author judgement: why the floor stays foreign in India</div>
<p>
My read, from the field. India's most software-influential sector is banking, and its
biggest buyers — public and private banks under RBI oversight — take a no-risk posture
even in pilots. They consume open source only through commercial, supported
distributions: RHEL not upstream Linux, OpenShift not upstream Kubernetes, never
Nomad, never StackUp. SBI, Indian Bank, Central Bank and others are publicly branded
for "open-source excellence" while running exclusively Red Hat's paid, de-risked
wrapper of that open source. China's policy
excludes foreign platforms and creates captive demand; India's local-content rules
reward badging and are defeated at the point of purchase by risk aversion.
</p>
<p>
And OSS contribution is itself a trust signal — a public proof of competence. 99Cloud
in OpenStack's top-10, China Telecom co-authoring ONAP: both advertise source-level
mastery no sales deck can fake. StackUp contributes little upstream, so a risk-averse
buyer has no such proof and defaults to the vendor that spent a decade advertising it.
</p>
<p>
[Hypothesis] If StackUp contributed materially to OpenStack, Indian buyers would take
it more seriously — not because the code would be better, but because the credential
would exist. The absence is self-reinforcing: no contribution, no credibility, no
sales, no resources to contribute.
</p>
</div>
#+end_export

* How Foundations Get Built
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: mechanism
:END:

Across the most load-bearing pieces of systems software, five development patterns
recur. Stated first, then evidenced against fifteen canonical projects.

| # | Pattern | China instance | India instance |
|---+---------+----------------+----------------|
| 1 | Lone obsessive rebuilds a tool judged wrong at the root | (rare; mostly firm-backed) | Goyal (Calibre, Kitty) — unaffiliated |
| 2 | Mass community, no single owner | growing (Gitee ecosystem) | thin |
| 3 | Target-bound backing: an institution commissions a named deliverable for a real user | delete-IOE mandate → OceanBase | mis-aimed at services export and defence hardware |
| 4 | Cross-domain carry: a specialist builds the tool their field lacks | present | rare |
| 5 | Forcing function: a scale problem that refuses the workaround | Alipay scale → OceanBase, RocketMQ | present, but buyers rent around it |

The fifteen projects each project traces to one or more of these patterns; none traces
to an elite teaching institution or a state programme aimed at a captive in-house user
— the two sources India has leaned on hardest.

|----------+--------------+---------|
| SQLite | one engineer, a Navy contract, then public domain | 1 |
| Linux | a student's hobby a community adopted | 1 → 2 |
| Git | Torvalds, two weeks, when a licence was pulled | 1 + 5 |
| Nginx | one engineer against one company's traffic wall | 1 + 5 |
| Redis | one founder, when his database could not keep up | 1 + 5 |
| PostgreSQL | a Berkeley research project handed to a community | 3 → 2 |
| Apache httpd | a group patching a server nobody maintained | 2 |

| Hadoop | two engineers on Google's papers, then Yahoo's backing | 5 + 3 |

* What a Foundational Move Requires
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: ecosystem
:END:

The origins in §9 name four conditions. They travel together.

| Condition | Meaning | China | India |
|-----------+---------+-------+-------|
| Forcing function | a real need that refuses the workaround | delete-IOE + platform scale | present, but buyers rent around it |
| Patient builders | people not on a fast exit from the problem | firms retained talent at home | elite graduates exit, or re-domicile |
| Neutral governance | a trusted home so a project outlives its author | Apache / CNCF at scale | almost none domestically |
| Community | many hands past the first commit | large, increasingly global | thin |

[Author judgement] India's failures here are of aim, not capacity: it built forcing
functions and pointed them at services export; built institutions and pointed them at
defence hardware. The conditions existed. They were aimed elsewhere.

* India's Handful
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: india-cases
:END:

Where the conditions did line up, India produced real foundational software. There is
a handful of it, and the scarcity is the evidence. Scale here is adoption — the reach
that separates a floor from a private tool.

| Project | What it does | Adoption |
|---------+--------------+----------|
| MOSIP[fn:mosip] | modular open-source digital-identity platform — the foundation a country builds its own Aadhaar-equivalent on | 10+ national deployments, ~130mn IDs issued |
| Frappe / ERPNext[fn:frappe] | metadata-driven web framework (Python, MariaDB): define an object's metadata and its UI, API, and permissions generate from it; ERPNext (700+ object types) is built on it | 22,000+ community; runs Zerodha's back office; among the most-forked open ERPs |
| Drove[fn:drove] | bare-metal container orchestrator PhonePe built when Kubernetes and Nomad did not fit its scale | ~350k cores, 3 PB at PhonePe — almost no adoption beyond it |

than any built at home. It is a tenant paying to maintain the building, generously, and
it is the clearest single illustration of the thesis.
</p>
</div>

#+begin_export html
<div class="story">
<div class="story-label">The story of Drove</div>
<p>
[Inferred] PhonePe outgrew off-the-shelf orchestration and built Drove, a container
orchestrator, running a bare-metal fleet of roughly 350,000 cores. It open-sourced it —
the right instinct. But whether Drove is bottom-up systems work or an assembly of
existing components is not established from the outside, so it should not be over-claimed
as a new primitive. Either way it has spread almost nowhere beyond PhonePe, because the
fourth condition — neutral governance and a community — was missing. China donated its
infrastructure to Apache and watched the world adopt it; India's best equivalents sit,
excellent and alone, in one firm's repositories.
</p>

MOSIP is the one that reached the world — because its forcing function came with a real
external user, a government with no identity system, that pulled it into the open. That
is the condition Drove lacked. India can build the floor. It has not built the machine
that makes floors spread.

* The Turn
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: turn
:END:

** The Elite-Industry Model Is Ending
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: elite
:END:

[Author judgement] India's theory of progress — concentrate the ablest in a few elite
institutions, expect breakthroughs — is aimed against the mechanism that produces
foundations. Elite institutions optimise for individual exit: the graduate who could
build a database has the best exit options in the country, and the trajectory points
outward or up to the application layer, never toward the patient, unpaid work of a
floor built at home. And the services firms meant to anchor the industry are precisely
what AI now marks down. The top-down model is losing both its engines at once.

** Knowledge Is Democratised; the Opening Is Bottom-Up
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: msme
:END:

The knowledge to build foundational software — papers, reference implementations,
weights, tooling — is now largely open. The gate the elite institutions held has
fallen. The decisive question is no longer /who has the knowledge/ but /who faces a
real problem with no budget to evade it/ — the forcing function itself.

| Model | Forcing function | Builders | Governance | Community |
|-------+------------------+----------+------------+-----------|
| Elite institute + large firm | weak (no real wall) | exit-bound | grant-based | insular |
| MSME cluster + local polytechnic | strong (real problem, no budget) | locally rooted | open by default | shared in-cluster |

Kerala did a version of this by accident (§6): free software in schools for lack of
money produced a disproportionate contributor base. The forcing function was poverty.

** The One Problem That Is India's Alone
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: language
:END:

[Author judgement] The sharpest target is the problem being rewritten by AI /and/
structurally India's to solve: language. Twenty-two official languages, a dozen
scripts, a billion people the current models serve badly. Leading open models handle
Hindi and little else; general tokenisers fragment Indic scripts so inefficiently that
every Indian model runs slower and costs more.[fn:sarvam] No American or Chinese firm
will fix this properly, because the pain is not theirs. The forcing function is real,
domestic, and un-outsourceable — the exact condition that, everywhere else here,
produced a foundation. The pieces exist, scattered across AI4Bharat, Sarvam, and
Bhashini; what is missing is the decision to consolidate them into the layer beneath
every Indian model.

* Foundational Targets, Mapped to MSME Clusters
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: msme-map
:END:

[Author judgement] A foundation gets built when three things meet: a real problem, no
budget to buy your way around it, and builders who are not on their way out of the
country. India's lesser-known industrial clusters supply all three. Each is a town or
region that makes one thing at national scale, sits next to an ordinary engineering
college, and runs into a software wall no foreign vendor will bother to fix.

| Foundational target | MSME cluster |
|---------------------+--------------|
| Indic-language OCR, voice, tokenisation | anchor near AI4Bharat, Chennai |
| Agri commodity price and quality data | Guntur — chilli |
| Kiln and furnace energy optimisation | Morbi — ceramics; Coimbatore — foundries |
| Offline, mixed-script logistics routing | Tiruppur — knitwear; Ludhiana — hosiery |
| Real-time machine-tool (CNC) control | Rajkot, Coimbatore |
| Supply-chain traceability for exports | Moradabad — brass; Kanpur — leather |

The captive problem behind each, in plain terms:

- *Indic language.* A billion people speak languages today's software still reads,
  hears, and types badly. The piece that breaks text into units for a model — the
  tokeniser — chops Indian scripts so clumsily that every Indian model runs slower and
  costs more. No American or Chinese firm will fix this, because the pain is not theirs.

- *Guntur chilli.* Guntur runs one of Asia's largest chilli markets. A farmer arriving
  with a truckload has no reliable way to know the fair price that day or to prove his
  crop's quality — how red, how pungent, how dry — so middlemen set both. There is no
  cheap tool that measures quality objectively and shows real market prices.

- *Morbi kilns.* Morbi makes most of India's ceramic tiles, fired in gas kilns where
  fuel is the single largest cost. Shaving a few percent off energy by tuning
  temperature and airflow would decide who stays profitable — but no affordable software
  does that optimisation for a small tile unit.

- *Tiruppur logistics.* In a dense knitwear town, thousands of tiny units ship to each
  other and outward, with addresses written in mixed Tamil, Hindi, and English and an
  internet connection that keeps dropping. Delivery software built for clean English
  addresses and always-on data simply fails here.

- *Rajkot CNC.* Rajkot's workshops cut metal on CNC machines whose control software —
  the program that tells the tool exactly how to move — is imported and expensive. A
  small unit cannot afford it, and no cheap Indian alternative exists.

- *Moradabad and Kanpur exports.* To sell brassware or leather abroad, a maker must
  prove the product is free of banned chemicals and trace every material back through
  its supply chain (Europe's REACH rules, US import checks). The traceability software
  to do this is priced for large exporters, so small ones are locked out.

Each wall sits beside a local polytechnic or NIT whose students need real problems and
are not bound for San Francisco. The problem cannot be bought around at the cluster's
price point — that is the forcing function. The builders are local and not exit-bound —
those are the patient hands. And because no market is large enough to tempt a
proprietary vendor, whatever gets built is open by default — that supplies the
governance and community. Four conditions, assembled where the elite model never looks.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

The chart that circulates every few months is not wrong about the numbers; it is wrong
about what they mean. India is a large, capable, application-layer software economy
that sells discounted labour and is, this year, watching that model meet its automator.
China is the deeper success — not because it has more engineers or better ones, but
because it chose to own the floor, manufactured the walls that force foundations into
existence, and did it until it had a stack.

The difference was never talent; India has supplied the world's foundational software
with its people for decades, just not at home. It was direction, and direction is a
choice, which means it remains available. The floor is being poured again this decade,
on the AI and language layers, and India stands on the one problem the rest of the
world has no reason to solve for it. The correction is to stop measuring success by how
many people work on someone else's foundation, and to go build one.

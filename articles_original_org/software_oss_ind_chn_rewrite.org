* The Two Phenotypes in One Browser
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: two-phenotypes
:END:

Open Google Chrome. Navigate to ~chrome://version~. The browser running
beneath that tab contains two distinct kinds of software, built by
utterly different processes, for utterly different reasons.

LevelDB sits inside Chrome's IndexedDB implementation and history
subsystem. It was written in 2011 by Jeff Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat —
two of the most celebrated engineers in the world, working at Google,
solving a problem Google had.[fn:1] It is excellent software. It is
institutional software: the product of abundant resources, clear
organisational mandate, and engineers whose careers were already secured.
The open-source release was a downstream decision, not the founding
impulse.

SQLite sits inside Chrome's cookie store, web storage, and preference
databases. It was written in 2000 by D. Richard Hipp, alone, for a
United States Navy contract to track asset status on a guided missile
destroyer.[fn:2] When the contract ended, he kept working on it. He
placed it in the public domain — no licence, no foundation, no
company — because that was the right thing to do. It now runs on
every Android phone, every iPhone, every macOS installation, every
Firefox and Chrome, in Airbus flight software, in every WhatsApp
installation on earth. One person. Public domain. Built because the
problem was interesting and the existing solutions were wrong.

The SQLite source tree contains this dedication:

#+begin_quote
May you do good and not evil. May you find forgiveness for yourself
and forgive others. May you share freely, never taking more than
you give.
#+end_quote

This is not a licence. It is a disposition. The question this article
asks is: which countries produce people with that disposition, and
which countries have systems that allow it to become software?

The standard answer to the question of who produces foundational
software points to the United States and, increasingly, China. It does
not point to India. The standard explanation is talent. This article
argues the standard explanation is wrong, that the real explanation is
structural, and that the structure is changeable.

* What Foundational Software Actually Is
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: foundational
:END:

Foundational software is not the application. It is not the service.
It is the layer that, if it disappears, everything above it stops
working. Databases. Operating systems. Network proxies. Message queues.
RPC frameworks. Consensus algorithms. Cryptography libraries.

The distinction matters because the country or company that controls
the foundational layer controls the terms on which everything above it
is built. This is not a metaphor. When Oracle raised database licence
fees in 2018, every enterprise on Oracle had a binary choice: pay or
rebuild. The ones who had built their own foundational layer had a
third option: nothing changes.

How does foundational software actually come about? The history is
instructive and consistently surprising.

JPEG was created by the Joint Photographic Experts Group — mostly
telecommunications engineers working on image compression for
transmission, not photographers or graphic designers.[fn:3] BLAS
(Basic Linear Algebra Subprograms) was written by numerical analysts
at Argonne National Laboratory solving physics simulation
problems.[fn:4] cuDNN — the CUDA library that makes modern deep
learning possible on Nvidia GPUs — was built by Nvidia engineers
solving a hardware utilisation problem, not by AI researchers.[fn:5]
The R programming language was written by two statisticians at the
University of Auckland, Ross Ihaka and Robert Gentleman, because they
needed a better tool for teaching statistics.[fn:6]

The pattern: foundational layers are almost always byproducts of depth,
not targets. Someone faces a problem that is too specific and too hard
for existing tools. They build something. The thing they build turns
out to be general. The generality was never the intention.

SQLite was not designed to be the world's most deployed database. It
was designed to track missile destroyer assets. The generality was a
byproduct of building it right.

This has direct implications for where foundational software comes
from. It does not come from research labs tasked with producing
foundational software. It comes from practitioners with real problems
and inadequate tools. Which means the question is not: /who has the
most talented engineers?/ The question is: /whose engineers face
problems that existing tools cannot solve?/

* China's Two Engines
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: china-engines
:END:

China built a full-stack alternative to US enterprise software
infrastructure in approximately one decade. This is not contested.
What is less understood is that it happened through two completely
different mechanisms operating simultaneously.

** Engine One: The Institutional Forcing Function

Alibaba's Singles' Day — November 11, China's largest retail event —
generated transaction volumes that no commercial database could handle
at any price.[fn:7] Oracle was not a viable solution at the peak loads
Alipay faced. The engineering team did not file a support ticket. They
built OceanBase, a distributed relational database written from scratch
in C++, now the holder of the TPC-C world record for transaction
throughput.[fn:8]

This is the institutional forcing function: a scale problem so
specific and so extreme that the only solution is to build the layer
that does not yet exist. Every component of Ant Group's SOFAStack
emerged this way. The service mesh MOSN was built because Envoy
could not handle Ant's proprietary Bolt protocol. The message queue
RocketMQ was built because IBM MQ could not provide the transactional
message semantics Alipay's payment flow required. The consensus
library SOFAJRaft was built because the available Java Raft
implementations could not meet latency requirements at production
scale.[fn:9]

The government reinforced this forcing function. China's IOE replacement
programme — targeting IBM servers, Oracle databases, and EMC storage
— converted expensive inconveniences into strategic liabilities after
2018 US trade restrictions.[fn:10] The 863 Programme, China's national
high-technology plan, funded specific engineering outcomes rather than
broad research: named technologies, funded teams, delivery
timelines.[fn:11] DARPA operates identically. Neither mechanism
produces research papers as the primary output. Both produce software.

** Engine Two: The Iconoclast

The institutional forcing function explains OceanBase. It does not
explain Gitea.

In 2014, a developer writing under the name 无闻 (Joe Chen) wanted a
lightweight, self-hostable alternative to GitHub.[fn:12] He built Gogs
— a Git hosting service in Go — alone, because the problem was
interesting and the existing solutions were either too heavy or required
trusting a third party with his code. Gogs now has over 44,000 GitHub
stars. Its fork, Gitea, is deployed at thousands of organisations
globally including engineering teams in Europe and North America who
have no awareness of and no interest in the Chinese technology
ecosystem. They use it because it is the best tool for the job.

TiDB, the distributed SQL database from PingCAP, started with three
engineers who had read the Google Spanner paper and decided to
implement it because the problem was interesting — not because there
was a validated market.[fn:13]

Monoio, ByteDance's Rust async runtime using Linux's io_uring interface,
was built because an engineer concluded that Tokio (the dominant Rust
async runtime) made the wrong architectural tradeoff for their
workload.[fn:14] That is the iconoclast's premise: the existing solution
is wrong at a level that cannot be fixed by configuration.

Both engines run simultaneously in China. The institutional machine
produces SOFAStack. The iconoclast produces Gitea. They do not require
each other. They do not coordinate. The output accumulates.

* India's Genuine Contribution: The Protocol Layer
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: india-protocol
:END:

India's contribution to open technology is real, globally significant,
and operates at a completely different layer from code. It operates at
the level of protocol and mandate.

The India Stack — Aadhaar, UPI, ONDC, DigiLocker — is a set of open
digital infrastructure specifications built on government mandate, with
private players competing on top.[fn:15] UPI is the clearest case.
NPCI defined the protocol, mandated zero transaction fees on
peer-to-peer payments, and forced every player to compete on
distribution and product rather than rent-extraction. The result:
14 billion transactions per month as of 2025, a model studied and
replicated in Singapore (PayNow), Brazil (Pix), and across a dozen
other countries.[fn:16]

This is a genuine and globally significant contribution. It must also
be precisely described. UPI is a specification, not deployable code.
PhonePe, Google Pay, and Paytm built proprietary implementations on
top of an open protocol. The protocol is open. The code is not.
ONDC goes further — it has open-source reference implementations on
GitHub for buyer apps, seller apps, and gateway nodes — making it
meaningfully closer to the code layer than UPI. But the dominant
implementations remain proprietary.

| Component | Type | Scale (2025) | What is actually open |
|-----------|------|-------------|----------------------|
| Aadhaar | Digital identity | 1.38B+ enrolled[fn:17] | Specification; not OSS code |
| UPI | Real-time payments | INR 24T+ monthly[fn:18] | Protocol spec; implementations proprietary |
| ONDC | Open commerce network | Growing; 14+ cities | Protocol spec + reference implementations |
| MOSIP | Identity platform (OSS) | 110M+ enrolled, 14 countries[fn:19] | Deployable open-source software |
| DigiLocker | Document wallet | 260M+ users | API specification |

MOSIP — the Modular Open Source Identity Platform, incubated at
IIIT-Bangalore — deserves separate treatment because it is software,
not merely specification. It takes the design principles of Aadhaar
and packages them as deployable code that any country can run without
purchasing from a Western biometric vendor. Active deployments in
Morocco, Philippines, Ethiopia, Nigeria, Peru.[fn:20] This is India's
closest analog to what Ant Group did with SOFAStack: build something
for a real domestic problem, package it for the world.

* The Talent Was Never the Constraint
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: talent
:END:

The engineers who designed AWS's core infrastructure, who wrote the
distributed systems running Google and Facebook, who built the databases
powering half the internet — many of them trained at Indian institutions.
The Kubernetes project has significant contributions from engineers of
Indian origin, employed by Google, Microsoft, and Red Hat.[fn:21] The
gRPC framework, now the dominant inter-service communication protocol
in cloud-native systems, was substantially built by engineers with
Indian educational backgrounds, working at Google.[fn:22] The V8
JavaScript engine — the runtime inside Chrome and Node.js — has Indian
contributors in its core team.

They built foundational software. They built it for American companies,
on American payrolls, for American users. India exported the talent.
America captured the infrastructure.

This is not a grievance. It is a structural observation. The engineers
made rational choices given their options. The problem is not that they
left. The problem is that the options available in India did not include
"work on something foundational." The options were: join an IT services
company, join a startup building on top of American infrastructure, or
leave.

The forcing function that might have created an Indian foundational
software industry — a scale problem that breaks existing tools, a
government programme that funds engineering outcomes rather than
research papers — simply did not exist. Indian technology companies
were structured from the beginning as services export businesses.
There was no Singles' Day equivalent. There was no existential vendor
dependency that could not be resolved by buying more Oracle licences.

* Why the Iconoclast Is Suppressed
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: suppressed
:END:

The institutional forcing function is a policy question. The iconoclast
is a cultural and systemic question, and the answer is harder.

** The Jugaad Trap

Indian engineering culture celebrates jugaad — the elegant workaround,
the frugal innovation, the good-enough solution assembled from existing
parts.[fn:23] This disposition produces extraordinary results at the
product layer. The Jaipur Foot, the Mitticool, the $800 electrocardiogram
— these are genuine achievements.

Jugaad is the antithesis of the foundational impulse. SQLite is not
jugaad. OceanBase is not jugaad. They are the result of someone
deciding that the existing solution is wrong at a level that cannot
be patched, and building a replacement from first principles. Jugaad
celebrates existing foundations. The iconoclast questions them.

** The Scientist-Programmer Who Does Not Exist in India

In Germany, a radiologist who wants to do computational analysis of
MRI scans will often learn the necessary programming, write a tool,
publish it, and it becomes part of the research ecosystem. This
is not unusual. The most widely used neuroimaging analysis tools on
earth were written by domain scientists, not computer scientists.

| Tool | Domain | Primary author(s) | Training | Country |
|------|--------|-------------------|----------|---------|
| SPM (Statistical Parametric Mapping) | Neuroimaging | Karl Friston | Psychiatrist | UK |
| FSL (FMRIB Software Library) | Neuroimaging | Stephen Smith et al. | Neuroscientists | UK |
| FreeSurfer | Neuroimaging | Bruce Fischl | Neuroscientist | US |
| R language | Statistics | Ross Ihaka, Robert Gentleman | Statisticians | New Zealand |
| Bioconductor | Genomics | Robert Gentleman et al. | Statistician / biologist | US |
| BLAST (sequence alignment) | Bioinformatics | Altschul et al. | Biologists | US |
| QGIS | Geospatial | Gary Sherman | Geographer | US |
| OpenFOAM | Fluid dynamics | Henry Weller et al. | Mechanical engineers | UK |
| GNU Octave | Numerical computing | John Eaton | Mechanical engineer | US |
| LilyPond | Music notation | Han-Wen Nienhuys | Mathematician | Netherlands |

The Indian column of this table is nearly empty. Not because India
lacks cardiologists, geographers, or fluid dynamicists. Because India's
professional education system treats domain expertise and computational
skill as mutually exclusive, and its institutional culture makes
exploration prohibitively expensive.

An MBBS student in India who spends time writing software is considered
to be wasting clinical study hours. There is no structural equivalent
of the American double-major or the European research institute where
biologists and computer scientists share lab space and problem sets.
More critically: a researcher who spends two years building a tool
that does not work loses their career in India — funding, position,
institutional standing. In Germany or the United States, they lose
two years. The failure cost asymmetry is not marginal. It is
decisive.

** CDAC and CDOT: Built Real Things, Lost the Feedback Loop

The standard response to India's foundational software gap is to note
that CDAC and CDOT exist. This is accurate. CDAC built the PARAM
supercomputer series and the GIST card that enabled Indian language
computing on PCs in the 1990s.[fn:24] CDOT built the RAX rural
telephone exchange, genuine indigenous telecom hardware.[fn:25] These
were not trivial achievements.

The problem was not capability. The problem was what happened after.

Every time CDAC produced a domestic alternative to a foreign product,
the procurement system found reasons to buy the foreign product
anyway. Procurement specifications were written — sometimes
deliberately, sometimes through institutional inertia — to match the
foreign vendor's feature set and certification requirements. The
corporate lobby operated not through corruption but through the far
more effective mechanism of specification capture: requirements that
only an incumbent can meet.

The second failure was retention. A good engineer at CDAC in 1998
could triple their salary by joining Infosys or Wipro. The
institutional knowledge left. What remained was process without
capability. OceanBase improved because Alipay's production load broke
it repeatedly and the team fixed it under pressure. CDAC's tools were
deployed in government contexts with no competitive pressure, no user
choice, and no feedback loop. They calcified.

* The Exceptions That Show the Path
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: exceptions
:END:

The iconoclast does exist in India. The examples are rare and largely
unsupported, but they are real, and they reveal the mechanism.

Kailash Nadh built Listmonk — a high-performance, self-hosted
newsletter and mailing list manager — not as a commercial venture but
as a labour of love born from a real need at Zerodha, the Indian
brokerage where he works.[fn:26] Zerodha could not find an adequate
open-source email list manager. Nadh built one. It is now used by
organisations globally, maintained in public, MIT-licensed, accepting
contributions from engineers who have never heard of Zerodha.

Santhosh Thottingal has spent years building foundational tools for
Malayalam computing — fonts, morphological analysers, transliteration
engines, hyphenation dictionaries — work that is unglamorous,
underfunded, and essential for anyone who needs software to work
correctly in one of India's major languages.[fn:27] He works at
Wikimedia Foundation. His Malayalam computing work is a labour of
love, not a job description.

Both cases share a structure: a CS-trained engineer, outside the
big-tech and elite-institute pipeline, facing a real problem for
which no adequate tool existed, building from the ground up, opening
the result, and maintaining it because the problem matters. This is
the SQLite phenotype. It exists in India. It is not the product of
policy or funding. It is the product of people who could not find the
tool they needed and were capable of building it.

The question is not whether this phenotype exists. It is why it
remains rare.

* The Organic Model: Thiruppatur, Not Silicon Valley
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: organic
:END:

Every standard proposal for fixing India's foundational software gap
points to the IITs and IISc, to big corporate R&D, to government
programmes. These are probably the wrong answers.

IIT graduates have excellent options. Google, Microsoft, and every
major US technology company recruit aggressively from top Indian
institutions. A student at IIT Delhi who chooses to build a
distributed database is passing up a comfortable and lucrative career
to do something difficult with uncertain reward. Most make the
rational choice. The incentive structure does not reward foundational
building at elite institutions.

The more promising candidate is the engineer at a B-grade polytechnic
in Thiruppatur or Nagpur or Bhubaneswar — a student with strong
technical foundations, fewer premium corporate options, and a problem
in their immediate environment that no existing software solves
adequately.

Consider Thiruppatur, a garment manufacturing cluster in Tamil Nadu.
A local transporter — 12 trucks, family-owned business — has a route
optimisation problem. The delivery addresses mix Tamil, transliterated
Tamil, and informal landmark references that Google Maps handles
poorly. No enterprise logistics software addresses this at a price
the transporter can pay. The local polytechnic has students who need
final-year projects. The transporter needs a tool.

The components of the foundational forcing function are all present:
a real problem, inadequate existing tools, technical capability nearby,
and an economics that makes proprietary software impossible. The only
component missing is the connective tissue that makes the output
visible beyond Thiruppatur.

The Kerala FOSS policy of the early 2000s shows that this connective
tissue can be lightweight and high-impact. When the Kerala government
mandated free and open-source software in schools — driven primarily
by budget constraints, not ideology — it created a generation of
engineers who built foundational tools for Malayalam computing that
are now used globally.[fn:29] Santhosh Thottingal is a product of
that ecosystem. The policy was not a grand vision. It was a budget
decision. The output was foundational software.

** Global Comparisons

Versions of this model exist elsewhere. The comparison is instructive.

| Model | Country | Mechanism | Output | What India can learn |
|-------|---------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Fraunhofer Institutes | Germany | Applied research institutes embedded in SME clusters; industry pays for problem-specific work; results often open | Deep SME-specific tools; several became foundational | The institute-cluster link is the key; not university, not corporate |
| CERN openlab | Switzerland/EU | Physics problem too large for any institution; forced open collaboration | WWW, ROOT framework, multiple scientific computing tools | Byproduct foundational software from real constraint |
| Embrapa | Brazil | Agricultural research tied to small farmer problems; software for specific crop and soil types | ALICE Web, domain-specific tools | Government research can produce usable software when the problem is real and specific |
| Kerala FOSS policy | India | State mandate for open-source in schools; budget constraint as driver | Malayalam fonts, input tools, Indic language computing | Already happened in India; replicable at state level |
| Taiwan SME cluster networks | Taiwan | Government-facilitated horizontal networks between SMEs in same cluster; shared tooling | Manufacturing execution systems, quality tools | The inter-cluster network is the missing piece |
| 863 Programme | China | Named engineering problems, funded teams, delivery timelines; not research grants | OceanBase, RocketMQ lineage, semiconductor progress | Outcome contracts, not research grants; applicable to software |

The failing model, for contrast, is LiMux — Munich's attempt to
replace Windows with Linux across city government, launched 2004,
abandoned 2017.[fn:30] The failure was not technical. It was that
the mandate came top-down without embedding in a real problem that
users needed solved. The software was adequate. The forcing function
was artificial. Artificial forcing functions produce compliance, not
software.

The inter-cluster connective tissue is the only piece requiring

* What India's Real Foundational Problems Are
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: india-problems
:END:

If the organic model is correct, the problems that could generate
foundational software are not in Bangalore. They are in the domains
where India's specific context makes existing tools inadequate.

*Indian language morphology at software depth.* Twenty-plus scripts,
sixteen hundred-plus dialects, agglutinative grammar that makes
tokenisation non-trivial, and virtually no production-grade open-source
tooling below the application layer. The person who builds a correct
Tamil morphological analyser, a proper Hindi compound-word segmenter,
or a Kannada syllabification engine is doing foundational work. This
tool does not exist at production quality. It needs to exist for Indian
language computing to work correctly. It will not be built by a US
company.

*Transaction graph analysis at UPI scale.* Fourteen billion
transactions per month is a graph problem — accounts as nodes,
transactions as edges — that no existing graph database was designed
for at that volume and at that real-time latency requirement. TuGraph
(Ant Group, C++) handles large graphs for fraud detection. It was
not designed for the specific topology of UPI's real-time
settlement graph. The team that builds a graph analysis engine
optimised for payment network topology, open-sourced it, and
benchmarked it against TigerGraph and Neo4j would be doing
foundational work with a clearly India-specific origin.

*Agricultural remote sensing pipeline.* ISRO generates satellite
imagery at resolutions and cadences that no commercial pipeline
currently processes end-to-end for smallholder agriculture decisions.
The image processing pipeline — from raw satellite data to actionable
per-field information for a farmer with a feature phone — does not
exist as open-source software. It requires domain knowledge (agronomy,
remote sensing, soil science) combined with software engineering.
This is precisely the scientist-programmer problem.

*Population genomics at Indian diversity scale.* India's genetic
diversity exceeds that of the entire continent of Europe.[fn:31] The
bioinformatics tools used by genomics researchers were calibrated on
European and East Asian reference populations. Analysis pipelines
that handle Indian population structure correctly, at scale, as open-
source software, do not exist. The researchers who need this are at
Indian institutions. The software engineers they need to work with
are at different Indian institutions, in different departments, with
no structural pathway to collaboration.

Each of these problems has the same shape as the problems that produced
JPEG, BLAS, and SQLite: too specific and too real for the existing
tools, requiring someone to build the layer that does not yet exist.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

The software myth runs as follows: China makes hardware, India makes
software. The reality is that China makes hardware and foundational
software. India makes services and protocols. Services are valuable.
Protocols are significant. Neither is the same as foundational software,
and the difference is not academic.

The database is the foundation. The operating system is the foundation.
The RPC framework, the service mesh, the consensus algorithm — these
are foundations. They are unglamorous, slow to build, invisible when
they work, and catastrophic when they fail or become unavailable. The
country or company that controls these layers controls the terms on
which everything above them is built.

China spent a decade building that control. It did so because it had
to. Singles' Day broke Oracle. US trade restrictions made the broken
Oracle a strategic liability. The engineers who built OceanBase were
not visionaries. They were engineers with a problem that no existing
tool could solve. They built the tool. The tool became foundational.

India's engineers are as capable. The problems exist. The Thiruppatur
transporter's address parsing problem, the UPI transaction graph, the
Tamil morphological analyser — these are real problems that require
foundational solutions and will not be solved by any American or
Chinese company. The iconoclast who would build them is somewhere in
a B-grade polytechnic in a tier-2 city, or at a bootstrapped Indian
company facing a real constraint, or working alone on a messaging
system that questions assumptions no one in the mainstream is willing
to question.

What that person needs is not primarily funding. It is the connective
tissue that makes their work visible — the norm of writing up what
you built before it is finished, the culture of benchmark transparency,
the inter-cluster network that lets Thiruppatur discover Tiruppur.

The SQLite motto — share freely, never taking more than you give —
is not a policy instrument. It is a disposition. Systems either reward
it or they do not. India's system is beginning to reward it, in
isolated pockets, in the people described in this article. Whether
those pockets remain isolated or connect into something larger is a
choice that can be made. It has not yet been made at scale.

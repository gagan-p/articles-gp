# 07 — Style Guideline
## Aman's Theory of the Rock

---

## Tone

The dominant register is Aman's: evidence-based, impatient with imprecision,
not unkind but not warm. The notebook entries are the only place he sounds
certain. Outside the notebook he is always mid-argument, always adjusting.

The prose surrounding him is precise but not literary. Place descriptions
are specific and earned — what is actually there, not what it represents.
Dialogue is true to each character's register. History emerges through
argument, not exposition.

**What the prose must never do**:
- Announce what a scene means before the scene has happened
- Explain a metaphor after using it
- Use "profound," "ancient," "timeless," or "sacred" as descriptors
- Describe a character's emotion and then have the character express the
  same emotion in dialogue
- End a chapter on a sentence that is trying to be the last line of the book

---

## Chapter Length

**Target**: 2,500–3,500 words per chapter. Maximum 4,000.
**Chapter 16 (flight home)**: 1,500 words maximum.
**If running long**: a scene is doing too much. Cut it.

---

## Scene Length

Most scenes: 300–600 words.
A scene over 800 words needs a reason. The reason is almost never "this idea
needs more space." It is sometimes "this conversation is genuinely going
somewhere new."

---

## Dialogue

No speech longer than five sentences from any character. If a character
needs more than five sentences to make a point, the point is not clear
enough yet. Cut until it is.

Two characters agreeing with each other produces no forward motion. Add
friction or cut one character's agreement.

---

## Description

Two to four sentences per location on first encounter.
One to two sentences on return visits.
The reader does not need the full landscape every time.

---

## Notebook Entries

- 80–120 words maximum. No exceptions.
- One observation, one gap, one question. That is all.
- Does not summarise the chapter.
- Does not repeat a metaphor the chapter already used.
- Placed at the end of the chapter, after the closing scene.
- No numbered questions after Ch 4. By mid-story the numbering is gone —
  Aman is inside the inquiry, not cataloguing its exterior.
- Evolution: Ch 1-4 structured and numbered. Ch 5-11 compressed, one
  observation plus one question. Ch 12-16 very short — sometimes two or
  three sentences, sometimes just one question without an answer.

---

## Dream Sequences

**No announcement.** The dream begins between one sentence and the next —
same paragraph, same prose register, no scene break, no white space before
it. The reader enters the dream when Aman does: without knowing it has started.

**How to signal the transition without announcing it**:
- A sensory detail that is wrong: the smell is different, the light is wrong,
  the ground is different under his feet
- A physical impossibility not flagged as impossible: he is standing when
  he was sitting; it is noon when it was dusk
- A detail too specific: the rouletted ware shard in the sand, a voice
  saying a particular thing

**Inside the dream**:
- Same prose register
- Historical characters speak plain English (understood as translation)
- They do not know they are in a dream
- They are doing ordinary work
- They find Aman's questions inconvenient

**Historical characters must NOT**:
- Deliver speeches summarising the historical argument
- Confirm Aman's theories
- Speak in proverbs or metaphors
- Be wiser than their historical position allows

**Dream length**: 400–600 words maximum.

**After the dream**: one paragraph of reorientation. What remains. What is
already gone. No immediate interpretation. The notebook entry for a
dream chapter is shorter than usual.

**Total dreams in the story**: four. No more.

| Dream | Chapter | Character |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Ch 7 | Palur dockworker |
| 2 | Ch 12 | Mahamatra of Samapa |
| 3 | Ch 13 | British surveyor |
| 4 | Ch 14 | Sadhaba merchant |

## Local Language Use

**Principle**: Odia, Hindi, Tamil and other languages appear as they appear
in the characters' actual lives — naturally, without translation for common
terms, without footnotes. Context teaches.

**Frequency**: Three to five Odia words or phrases per chapter maximum.
The story is in English. The Odia is evidence that English is a translation
of something else.

**Established terms — use consistently, do not re-explain**:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| *Koko ra pua* | Koko's son (Gagan) |
| *Koko ra nati* | Koko's grandson (Aman) |
| *Kanchi ghar* | The old thatched house |
| *Khani* | Earthen rice storage structure |
| *Khanipaka* | Rice matured in the khani; worth more for being underground |
| *Amba ghar* | House of the mango |
| *Agula* | Low-lying water catchment |
| *Gamcha* | Light cotton wrap/towel |
| *Vrat* | Women's ritual fast/observance |
| *Tirtha* | Pilgrimage site |
| *Mahamatra* | Ashokan administrative officer |
| *Rajavacanika* | The royal reader at Jaugada |
| *Dhaba* | Roadside eatery |
| *Jatra* | Odia folk theatre form |
| *Gaan Karan* | Village headman/administrator |

**Terms needing no explanation**: puja, dal, upma, idli, chai, lungi,
kurta, saree. The reader knows these or infers them.

**How to handle translation**: Do not write dialogue twice. Choose one
language. If a specific Odia phrase matters, have a character use it inside
otherwise English dialogue.

---

## Chapter Structure

**Standard structure**: Three to four scenes per chapter. Each scene has
one primary thing happening.

**Scene transitions**: White space (section break). No transitional sentences
of the form "later that afternoon." The next scene begins in the middle of
the next thing.

**Opening lines**: Start in the middle of something. An action, a line of
dialogue, a specific detail. Not "the next morning." Not a landscape
description. The landscape can come after — two sentences, once we are
already there.

**Closing lines**: Something specific and small. A physical detail. A line
of dialogue. Not a thematic summary. The chapter does not need to know
what it meant. The reader decides.

---

## The Philosophical Overdose Check

Count before filing any chapter:

| Item | Limit |
|------|-------|
| Thesis statements by characters | 2 per chapter maximum |
| Metaphors used | 2 per chapter, different from each other |
| Notebook entry repeating what chapter already said | 0 |
| Character saying something that sounds like a chapter heading | 0 |
| Times "cage" appears | 1 per chapter (zero is fine) |
| Sona ma appearances | 1 in the whole story |
| Nanda appearances | 1 in the whole story |

If any count is exceeded: cut before filing.

---

## The One-Sentence Test

After writing a chapter, complete this sentence:
*In this chapter, _______ happens and _______ changes.*

If you cannot complete it: the chapter has no shape yet.
If both blanks need more than ten words: the chapter is doing too much.

---

## The Aman Test

Read Aman's dialogue aloud after writing it. If it sounds like a historian,
rewrite it. He is fifteen. He gets things wrong and corrects himself and
gets annoyed when he has to correct himself. He does not summarise.
He argues.

---

## What No Chapter Needs

- A recap of what was established in previous chapters
- A character explaining why a place is historically significant before the
  place has shown it
- A scene that exists only to deliver historical information with no dramatic
  function
- Two characters agreeing with each other at length
- A closing line that contains an abstract noun (truth, time, history,
  memory, permanence)

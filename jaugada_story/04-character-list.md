## Primary Characters

### Aman
- Age 15. Born Chennai, raised Kuala Lumpur.
- Languages: Odia (functional, accented — he knows it and it sometimes matters),
  English (primary), Hindi, French (studied), Mandarin (studied).
- Addresses parents as Baba (Gagan) and Mama (Minati).
- Personality: argues from evidence, impatient with imprecision. Will pursue
  an argument through inconvenience. Does not sentimentalise. Does not perform
  curiosity — he is actually curious.
- Currently reading: *The Story of Paper*, Jonathan Bloom (2001). Finishes
  it around Ch 8-9.
- The notebook: titled *Theory of the Rock*. One entry per chapter. The only
  place he sounds certain.
- His Odia: functional but accented. Village people sometimes slow down for
  him. He registers this.
- Voice: short sentences. Evidence first. Corrects himself openly. Will stop
  mid-argument to say "that's not quite right" about his own position.

### Gagan
- Aman's father. Born and raised Bada Agula, Ganjam.
- Known in Bada Agula as *Koko ra pua* (Koko's son). Aman is *Koko ra nati*.
- Studied physics (Berhampur/Bhubaneswar), then IFMR Chennai. Now independent
  researcher and writer, based Kuala Lumpur.
- Not a historian and knows it. Calibrated confidence — hedges when he should.
- Personality: precise, dry, occasionally lapses into lecture mode and catches
  himself. Patient with Aman but not infinitely so.
- Role: pacing check on Aman's theorising. Provides the "I'm simplifying"
  and "that's more complicated than that" correction.
- Voice: self-corrects openly. Dry humour, rarely deployed.

### Minati
- Aman's mother. From the Bhanjanagar region. Dentist, practice split KL
  and Chennai.
- Languages: Odia, Hindi, English, Tamil, Gujarati, Punjabi; understands
  some Malayalam and Telugu.
- Personality: waits until she has something that cuts. When she speaks
  in an argument, it lands harder than what came before it, precisely because
  she has been quiet.
- Clinical habit: what's beneath the surface, what's been repaired badly,
  what's been ignored until it became a bigger problem.
- Her personal connections to the sites: her grandmother walked to Jaugada
  for a vrat (Ch 2). Her mother knows the Tapoi story as something women
  carry (Ch 6).
- Voice: fewer words than the men. Her analogies come from dentistry —
  not performed, just how she thinks.

### Nabin (Koko)
- Gagan's father. Age 71. Lives in Berhampur with Padmini.
- White Maruti, immaculate. First graduate from Bada Agula.
- Pitambara was his grandfather.
- Personality: thinks first, speaks after. Treats Aman as a person to
  talk to directly. Comfortable with silence.
- Holds the last sheet from Pitambara's bag. Has read it. Will not share
  it until Ch 15.
- Voice: sparse. When he says something it has been considered. Does not
  explain himself.

### Padmini
- Nabin's wife. Gagan's mother. Aman's paternal grandmother.
- Satyabadi Pattanayak's granddaughter.
- Lives in Berhampur with Nabin throughout the trip.
- Keeps Satyabadi's book (*Rath Nuhen Bhageerath*, Bani Prakshan, Polasara)
  on a shelf in the house. Does not foreground it. It is simply her
  grandfather's writing.
- Her register: quieter than Nabin in the academic conversations. Practical.
  Manages the house. The one who arranged the food, the borrowed wire, the
  domestic logistics of the visit without this being made into a joke.
- When Aman finds or asks about the book, she is the one who says what it
  is and who Satyabadi was — briefly, without ceremony.
- She knows: he was the Gaan Karan of Tentulia. He was an Arya Samaji.
  He was famous and learned. He died of leprosy, untouched, as was the
  norm of those days. He died uncelebrated. The book is what is left.

## Expert Character

### Achintya Raut
- Gagan's friend from university. Teaches at Tezpur Central University,
  Assam. From Hinjilikatu, Ganjam.
- Back in Odisha for Dussehra. Arrives at Bada Agula Day 2 on a battered
  Pulsar with a cracked mirror. Travels with the family through Ch 13.
  Returns to Assam from Bhubaneswar; available by phone after that.
- Family background: grandfather was a clerk in the British-period revenue
  system. Family habit: read everything, trust nothing you haven't checked.
- Personality: precise, unhurried. States what he knows and why he might
  be wrong. Attacks the theory's weaknesses, not the person. Does not
  perform expertise.
- Role: the test of the lining. When Aman develops a theory, Achintya
  identifies where the lining is weak.
- Voice: careful, qualified sentences. "The evidence is ambiguous and anyone
  who tells you otherwise is selling something."

## Site Characters — One Appearance Each

### Nrusingha (Ch 1, Ch 2, briefly Ch 15)
- Heavyset, sixties, from Baliguda village near Jaugada. Comes to the site
  most mornings. Thermos of tea on the flat stone.
- One established line: "Rocks have very good publicists."
- Knows the corridor logic. Observes, doesn't lecture.

### Nanda (Ch 4)
- Alekh Akhada, Bada Agula. One appearance.
- One point: a stone can preserve a lie as well as a truth. Better, in
  fact. Because a lie, once carved, cannot correct itself.
- Does not reappear.

### Sona ma (Ch 4)
- Very old, Bada Agula village edge. One appearance. Answers in song.
- One song. Does not reappear.

### Temple priest (Ch 5, Athagada)
- Fifties, sweeping the flagstones. Tells the Kalapahada story without drama.
- One line that matters: "They found all of them. The idols came back.
  Some took longer than others."

### Minati's father (Ch 6, Bhanjanagar)
- Retired government servant. Mentions the Ghumusar wars over dinner the
  way you mention weather. One contribution: the administration adjusted
  its maps; the hills didn't adjust.

### Minati's mother (Ch 6, Bhanjanagar)
- Tells the Tapoi story the way you tell something everyone locally knows.
- One line: "The men went. The tulsi got kept. That's what the story keeps."

### Prasanna (Ch 10, Palur)
- OIMSEAS archaeologist, mid-thirties, practical.
- One line: "The shard says: I was made, I was used, I was broken, I ended
  up in the ground here. That's all it says."

### The mosque caretaker (Ch 10, Potagarh)
- Keeps objects fishermen bring on a shelf inside the Masjid entrance.
- Refuses institutional removal: "If I give this to a museum, there is
  no more place for the fishermen to bring things."
- Right, as Aman argues: the shelf is the provenance.

### The net man (Ch 11, Potagarh beach)
- Mending a net below the fort's seaward wall. Generations of this coast.
- Tells the Vijaya oral tradition — prince banished from Kalinga, sailed
  to an island, became king; something sacred taken, connected to his
  dishonour. Does not know which tradition the sacred thing belonged to.

### Subrat (Ch 11, Brahmapur)
- Nabin's cousin's son. Kept the tin trunk without opening it.
- Practical man with the family obligation habit.

## Dream Characters — Four Dreams, No More

These characters are doing ordinary work. They find Aman's questions
inconvenient. They do not deliver speeches.

### The Palur dockworker (Dream 1, Ch 7)
- Loading lac sacks onto coastal boats. Pre-Ashoka-as-emperor.
- Key line: "Bindusara's son has the throne. Since the succession."
- On who built the fort at Samapa: "This beach. These boats. The lac.
  Kings come and go. The lac doesn't care."

### The Mahamatra of Samapa (Dream 2, Ch 12)
- Middle manager at the Samapa administrative post. Receiving the edict
  text. Has a tax inventory to finish.
- Reads "all men are my children" with the expression of a man reading
  a company values statement printed on a breakroom wall.
- Does not know he is in a dream. Finds Aman's presence unremarkable —
  in dreams, presence is not strange.

### The British surveyor (Dream 3, Ch 13)
- 1870s-1880s, doing the measurement work that Beglar published.
- Explains with total unselfconsciousness why the Raj needed a good Indian
  emperor — what the "benevolent Buddhist king" story permits, what it
  closes off.
- Not a villain. A man inside his own ideology, which is invisible to him.

### The Sadhaba merchant (Dream 4, Ch 14)
- Pre-departure, Kartik Purnima. Loading a boat at the coast.
- Has heard of a teacher in the west whose students travel with merchants.
  Finds this unremarkable: monks and traders have always moved together.
  It is practical.

## The Recurring Motif (Not a Character)

### The Kalinga Jina
Never fully explained. Never identified with the copper disc.
- A sacred Jain image taken from Kalinga by the Nanda dynasty.
- Held in Pataliputra through Chandragupta's and Ashoka's reigns.
- Recovered by Kharavela, ~150 years after Ashoka's death.
- Source: Hathigumpha inscription of Kharavela, Udayagiri, 1st century BCE.
- Three mentions: Ch 3 (Achintya, academic), Ch 13 (Jain pilgrim, devotional),
  Ch 15 (village elder, family memory, second-hand).

## Historical Presences

### Pitambara (deceased)
- Gagan's paternal great-great-grandfather. Congress man. Karan scribe.
- Active 1920s-1940s. Attended Congress meetings in Cuttack, walking to
  Purushottampur, transport to Berhampur, train to Cuttack.
- 1929: took minutes at a district-level Congress committee discussing
  whether Ganjam should be included in the proposed Odia province.
- 1934: drafted argument that Jaugada's Ashokan edict proves Ganjam is
  ancestrally Odia and belongs in the Odia province. Used the same rock
  Ashoka used — to claim the territory back.
- 1 April 1936: wrote something on the day Ganjam transferred to Odisha.
  The last sheet in the bag. Contents revealed Ch 15.
- Papers: 17 sheets, 1924-1936. In a tin trunk in Brahmapur.

### Satyabadi Pattanayak (deceased)
- Padmini's grandfather. Gagan's maternal great-great-grandfather on his
  mother's side.
- Gaan Karan (village headman) of Tentulia village, Ganjam.
- Arya Samaji: rationalist, reformist Hindu tradition; opposed to idol
  worship, caste ritual, and religious superstition; believed in reason
  and social reform.
- Wrote *Rath Nuhen Bhageerath* (Not the Chariot, But Bhagirath): a story
  set in the Athagada area around the Rath Yatra tradition; uses the
  Bhagirath myth to argue that extraordinary achievement comes from
  ordinary persistent effort, not from the vehicle of inherited power.
- Published by Bani Prakshan, Polasara, Ganjam.
- Died of leprosy. Untouched in his final period, as was the norm of
  those days. Social isolation in illness, even for a man of standing.
- Famous and learned. Died uncelebrated.
- His book is on a shelf in Padmini's keeping in Nabin's Berhampur house.
  Always there. Never hidden. Never read by the people who walked past it.

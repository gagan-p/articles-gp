## Ch 1 — Why Here?

**Themes to Explore**
- The fort predates Ashoka: the question is what was here, not why Ashoka
  chose it
- The transit point logic: this place matters because of the road, not
  because of what it produces
- The missing name: Ashoka names five Greek kings but not the king of Kalinga

**Themes to Allude**
- The trade corridor (to be developed Ch 7)
- Silk-weaving families near Pandia — older tradition than Berhampur's Dera
  community (never fully developed; noted once)

**Geography**
- Pandia/Jaugada village, Ganjam district
- The Malati range granite outcrops
- The earthen rampart: 858 x 814 yards, visible across the paddy fields
- The Rushikulya in the distance to the east

**History**
- J.D. Beglar survey 1882: 858 x 814 yards, 18 feet high, 8 gates, moat
- Pre-Mauryan punch-marked coins and pottery confirmed in excavation
- Edict XIII absent from Jaugada and Dhauli; present at 14 other sites
- Greek kings named in Edict XIII: Antiochus II, Ptolemy II, Antigonus
  Gonatas, Magas of Cyrene, Alexander of Epirus — none of their records
  mention Ashoka independently

**Links to Other Chapters**
- The notebook opened here underpins every subsequent chapter
- The corridor logic (Nrusingha) is confirmed physically at Ch 5 (Athagada
  hills), expanded at Ch 7 (lac thread), confirmed in the edict's own text
  at Ch 3
- The missing king's name is revisited at Ch 3 (Achintya) and Ch 9 (theory
  weaknesses)

**Themes to Explore**

**Themes to Allude**
- The Greek silence (mentioned once, not returned to)
- Dhauli as the senior node (to be physically confirmed at Ch 12)

**Geography**
- Jaugada compound: the cage, the rock face

**History**
- Edicts 1-10 and 14 at Jaugada; Edicts 11, 12, 13 absent

  Ashoka in Greek records

## Ch 5 — Athagada

**Themes to Explore**
- The pattern of sacred objects taken, hidden, and recovered — without
  claiming connection to the Kalinga Jina
- Writing in a marginalised language as an act of identity assertion:
  Kabisurya Baladev Rath writing Odia when the administration preferred Telugu
- The zamindari as the administrative unit that contained the family's land

**Themes to Allude**
- The hill country as the permanently unconquered territory (Ghumusar; the
  Samapa edict's "unconquered territories beyond the borders")
- The keys in the kanchi ghar box — the Athagada zamindari court as the
  possible address for the unidentified keys

**Geography**
- Athagada Patna: ~62km from Ganjam town, Ganjam district
- The 16th century Jagannath temple built in the hills, cluster of temples,
  mountain framing the shrine
- Kabisuryanagar: 10km from Athagada, named for the poet
- The hill country visible to the west: the Ghumusar range

**History**
- Kalapahada: military commander of Sulaiman Karrani of Bengal, 16th century;
  attacked temples across Odisha; the Athagada king gave shelter to the
  Gajapati king of Puri and hid the Jagannath idol in the hills
- Kabisurya Baladev Rath: 1789-1845, born Athagada; Odia language poet;
  wrote in Odia during the period the Madras Presidency administration
  preferred Telugu for official use
- Athagada zamindari: contained Bada Agula; auctioned 1854, bought by
  Khallikote zamindar

**Links**
- The idol-hiding pattern rhymes with the Kalinga Jina (noted in notebook,
  not argued)
- Kabisurya's Odia writing connects to Pitambara's Congress minutes (Ch 11)
  and Satyabadi's story (Ch 4, Ch 15): same argument, different forms
- The hill country here visually connects to the Ghumusar wars mention
  at Ch 6 and the "unconquered territories" of the Samapa edict (Ch 3)

## Ch 8 — The Drunk Man

**Themes to Explore**

**Geography**

  at the third granite outcrop from the bottom
- Forest Department rest house near Surada: one bulb, a wooden table

**History**

**Links**

## Ch 10 — What the Excavation Found

**Themes to Explore**
- The stupa as commercial signal: the Buddhist trade network used port-stupas
  as trust-signals and navigational landmarks
- The signal may not have been physical: reputation cannot be excavated
- The shelf as provenance: removing the copper disc from the mosque caretaker's
  keeping destroys the only context it has

**Themes to Allude**
- The copper disc as possible (but never claimed) Kalinga Jina: the rhyme
  is there for readers who want it; the text refuses it

**Geography**
- Palur village, Ganjam: near Rambha, near the Rushikulya mouth, the
  ancient port
- The hilltop above the port: where the stupa is
- Potagarh: star-shaped plan, two pillars still standing at the landward
  gate, old Masjid intact inside the compound, the caretaker's shelf

**History**
- 2023 OIMSEAS excavation at Palur:
  - Buddhist stupa: rectangular platform 12m x 3m, rising 5.5m; dated
    approximately 1st-2nd century CE
  - Rouletted ware sherds: fine ware with regular stamped decoration;
    distribution across the Bay of Bengal (Sri Lanka, Malaysia, Indonesia)
    predates the stupa; not Roman import, locally produced
  - Amphora pieces; clay bullae with Roman Silenus head imagery
- Potagarh history: Qutb Shahi period (~1641 CE), then Edward Costford
  first British Resident of Ganjam, construction/expansion 1768; first
  Collectorate; moved to Berhampur 1815 due to epidemic
- The copper disc: Jain kayotsarga posture (Tirthankara formal standing
  position); marks possibly Brahmi but worn; possibly early historic period
  (3rd to 1st century BCE); cannot confirm without XRF analysis

**Links**
- The rouletted ware shard weight connects to the Palur dream shard (Ch 7)
- The copper disc's kayotsarga figure rhymes with the Kalinga Jina without
  being claimed as it (thread at Ch 3, Ch 13, Ch 15)
- The stupa-as-signal hypothesis underpins the broader Buddhist trade network
  thread (alluded throughout, never fully developed)

## Ch 13 — The City With No Burnt Layer
*Dream 3: British surveyor*

**Themes to Explore**
- Sisupalgarh's no destruction layer: the structure survived the conquest
  intact; the city kept running; power changed hands without the city being
  destroyed
- The "great emperor" story has recent authors: the British surveyor dream
  shows that the Ashoka narrative was constructed for the Raj's purposes
- Kharavela's inversion: where Ashoka named five Greek kings but not his
  Kalingan enemy, Kharavela named exactly what was taken and who took it

**Themes to Allude**
- The Kalinga Jina #2: the Jain pilgrim's passing reference at Khandagiri
- The Ajivika tradition: Dasaratha (Ashoka's grandson) dedicated the
  Barabar caves to the Ajivikas — the dynasty did not produce a clean
  Buddhist lineage

**Geography**
- Sisupalgarh: near Bhubaneswar airport; the octagonal plan visible in
  the earthworks; trees growing through the walls
- Khandagiri/Udayagiri: rock-cut caves, Jain pilgrimage site; the
  Hathigumpha on the hilltop behind iron grilles; the inscription's
  damaged surface

**History**
- Sisupalgarh: B.B. Lal excavation (1948); later work Monica Smith; octagonal
  plan; occupation 7th-6th century BCE to 4th century CE — continuous;
  no destruction layer; identified with Tosali (Ashoka's Kalinga capital)
  and/or Kalinganagara (Kharavela's capital); the same walls served both
- Hathigumpha inscription of Kharavela (1st century BCE): records recovery
  of the Kalinga Jina from the Nanda/Maurya possession; partial damage to
  inscription; some readings contested
- British surveyor dream: Beglar-era (1870s-1880s), did the measurements
  published in the Archaeological Survey reports; the Raj needed a "good"
  Indian emperor — a benevolent Buddhist king — to justify and frame its
  own administrative self-image; this was ideological, unconscious, total

**Links**
- No destruction layer here connects to no battle evidence at Dhauli (Ch 12):
  the ground does not confirm the war
- Kalinga Jina #2 (Jain pilgrim) connects to #1 (Ch 3, Achintya) and
  #3 (Ch 15, village elder)
- The British surveyor dream connects to Pitambara's papers (Ch 11): the
  same rock (Jaugada), used by the British for one narrative and by Pitambara
  for the opposite narrative

# 01 — Premise and Goal
## Aman's Theory of the Rock

---

## The Central Question

Why would a king place his edicts in what is today a small, obscure village
in Ganjam district, Odisha?

This question is asked by a fifteen-year-old boy standing in front of a
rusted ASI signboard and an iron cage around a thirty-foot wall of Brahmi
script in a place called Pandia (officially Jaugada), with a goat on the
earthen rampart and a tea stall forty metres away.

---

## The Story in One Paragraph

Aman, fifteen, raised in Kuala Lumpur, stands at Jaugada and asks: why here?
The attempt to answer takes him and his family through three weeks in Ganjam —
upstream to the source of the Rushikulya river, downstream to its mouth on the
Bay of Bengal, north to the sites of a war that left no bones, and back. By
the end he does not have an answer. He has a better question, written in a
notebook that may or may not survive the next forty years, about a rock that
has already survived two thousand.

---

## Premise

A family returns to Ganjam, Odisha for Dussehra:

- **Gagan**: Aman's father. From Bada Agula. Independent researcher. Physics
  background. Not a historian.
- **Minati**: Aman's mother. Dentist. From the Bhanjanagar region. Quieter
  than Gagan in argument; lands harder when she speaks.
- **Aman**: Their son. Fifteen. Born Chennai, raised Kuala Lumpur. Argues
  from evidence. Keeps a notebook titled Theory of the Rock.

What begins as a family holiday to the ancestral village becomes, through
Aman's systematic questioning, a journey through the Rushikulya valley and
the two-thousand-year argument still being made in granite, paper, and ink.

---

## What the Story Argues (Provisionally)

Aman's theory is not settled by the end. What it arrives at:

1. Jaugada was important before Ashoka — pre-Mauryan coins, pre-existing
   fortification, trade corridor to the sea
2. The Separate Kalinga Edicts are administrative instruments addressed to
   frontier officers, not confessions to the conquered people
3. Edict XIII (the confession) went everywhere except Kalinga — for external
   audiences, not for the people of Kalinga
4. The Kalinga War may have been the acquisition of a trade network rather
   than the defeat of a named king — held as labelled hypothesis
5. The Kalinga Jina was in Mauryan possession throughout Ashoka's reign and
   was never returned
6. The same pattern repeats: Mauryan administration → British Madras
   Presidency → zamindari period → Pitambara's Congress committee → Aman's
   notebook. Head office far away. Foreign script. Local compliance required.
7. What survives is not what was most important but what was most durable
8. The argument in the rock is still being used — by Pitambara in 1934,
   by Aman in 2024

---

## The Governing Logic of the Journey

The Rushikulya river is the spine. The family follows it upstream to its
source, downstream to its mouth, then leaves it deliberately to go north.
Leaving the river is registered. The river does not need anyone to follow it.

---

## What the Story Is Not

- Not a romanticisation of Ashoka as saint or pacifist
- Not a textbook in narrative form
- Not magical realism — the dreams are always possibly just dreams
- Not condescending to oral tradition — treated as evidence of belief,
  which is a different but equally valid category of evidence from epigraphy
- Not a definitive answer to the central question
- Not a claim that the Kalinga War did not happen
- Not a claim that the copper disc is the Kalinga Jina
- Not a claim that Vijaya was specifically from Ganjam

The story ends with better questions, not answers.
